## HEADLINE SUMMARY

- 406,597 hectares of mangrove forest cover Mozambique's coastline - a national natural capital asset with no systematic condition monitoring in place.
- Two southern study sites (Save River Delta and Morrumbene Estuary) hold a combined 80,330 Mg of carbon - equivalent to 294,700 Mg of CO2. These figures are based on aboveground biomass only; belowground soil carbon has not yet been measured and may account for a further 50-60 percent of total ecosystem carbon.
- Save River Delta holds aboveground carbon stocks that, if registered under a voluntary carbon market framework with an MRV system in place, could generate an estimated USD 275,000-550,000 per year - a minimum estimate, as belowground soil carbon has not yet been measured.
- Both sites show canopy heights of around 5 metres against a reference of 15 metres for intact East African mangroves - structural degradation that is now quantified and policy-ready.
- The SEEA EA condition account establishes the independent scientific baseline required for blue carbon MRV under Verra VM0033 - the same standard used by Africa's largest mangrove carbon project - and positions Save and Morrumbene as investment-grade pipeline assets for Mozambique's ProAzul blue finance facility.

## SITUATION

### The regional picture

Mangrove forests are among the most productive and economically important ecosystems along the Western Indian Ocean coast. They support artisanal fisheries, protect coastal communities from storm surges and erosion, and store substantial quantities of carbon in their biomass and soils - with mangrove ecosystems sequestering carbon at approximately ten times the rate of tropical terrestrial forests. For WIO coastal states - from Somalia to South Africa - mangroves underpin the food security and climate resilience of millions of people who depend directly on coastal resources.

Despite this, most WIO governments manage mangroves without quantified baselines. Policy decisions on aquaculture siting, timber harvesting, coastal zone planning, and climate finance applications proceed without standardised data on forest extent, structural condition, or carbon stocks. The result is a persistent gap between political commitments to mangrove conservation - reflected in national NDCs, RAMSAR designations, and blue economy strategies - and the evidence base needed to implement, monitor, or enforce those commitments.

This gap also represents a significant market constraint. Fewer than 15 major blue carbon projects globally are currently capable of issuing verified sequestration credits. Supply is critically scarce relative to growing corporate and sovereign demand for high-integrity nature-based removals. Each new credible baseline established in the WIO region therefore carries significance beyond its local context - it is potential entry into a market where supply cannot meet demand.

This brief presents findings from the first ecosystem account for Mozambique's southern mangroves, produced under the UN SEEA EA framework using 2024-2025 satellite observations. Ecosystem accounts convert natural assets into the same standardised language as national economic statistics - enabling governments to track the condition of forests, fisheries, and coastal systems the same way they track GDP or trade balances. The methods and approach are transferable across the WIO region, and Mozambique's experience can serve as a model for neighbouring states.

### The Mozambique context

Mozambique holds 406,597 hectares of mangrove forest - one of the largest national extents in sub-Saharan Africa. Northern provinces dominate: Sofala Province alone accounts for 39.7 percent of the national total. Southern mangroves, concentrated in Gaza and Inhambane provinces, face mounting pressure from aquaculture expansion, freshwater disruption from upstream dam operations, coastal erosion, and informal timber harvesting.

Mozambique already hosts Africa's largest mangrove carbon project. MozBlue, operating in Zambézia Province along the Zambezi River Delta, is developing 30,000 or more hectares of mangrove restoration under a 60-year operating license, using the Verra VM0033 methodology and financing through Verified Carbon Units. By early 2026 approximately 1,300 hectares had been restored and 10 million trees planted. The project structure - a tripartite benefit-sharing arrangement between developer, government, and local communities channelled through community fishing councils - has become the operational model for blue carbon in Mozambique.

The Save and Morrumbene sites represent the next phase of that geographic expansion southward. They are not part of MozBlue. They are not yet registered carbon projects. But they occupy the same policy and market logic: mangrove ecosystems with measurable carbon stocks, community land rights requiring formal benefit-sharing arrangements, and an institutional framework - ProAzul - actively seeking to connect sites like these with international investors. Establishing the southern baseline is the prerequisite for entering that pipeline.

Two sites were selected as the pilot study areas for this baseline assessment:

- Save River Delta (Gaza Province): 12,085 hectares. The largest southern mangrove complex, spanning the Gaza-Sofala provincial boundary. Characterised by heterogeneous structure - a mosaic of productive patches and degraded areas. Aquaculture expansion in the surrounding area has been growing at 5-10 percent per year.
- Morrumbene Estuary (Inhambane Province): 1,677 hectares. A smaller, uniformly stressed mangrove system. Evidence from satellite observations points to freshwater deficit as one possible driver. Satellite data are consistent with a freshwater deficit as one possible cause; upstream river regulation is one plausible explanation that warrants hydrological investigation before any attribution is made to specific infrastructure or cross-boundary factors.

Together, these study sites cover 13,761 hectares, or 3.4 percent of the national mangrove total. They represent the southernmost significant mangrove ecosystems in Mozambique and some of the most exposed to documented human pressure.

Coastal communities along both coastlines hold customary occupation rights and formal DUAT allocations under the Land Law (Law No. 19/1997). They depend on these mangrove systems for subsistence fishing, fuel wood, and coastal protection. Any carbon credit or conservation initiative that affects these areas must establish community consultation and benefit-sharing arrangements consistent with those rights before implementation.

## KEY FINDINGS

### Finding 1: Both sites are structurally degraded relative to their potential

Using NASA GEDI satellite LIDAR observations collected between 2024 and 2025, mean canopy heights were measured at 5.07 metres (Save) and 5.27 metres (Morrumbene). The reference level for intact East African estuarine mangrove is 15 metres. This places both sites at condition indices of 0.17-0.19 on a 0-1 scale - meaning they are functioning at roughly one-fifth of their structural potential.

Aboveground biomass averages 12.63 Mg/ha at Save and 10.95 Mg/ha at Morrumbene. Canopy cover is extremely low at 8.8 percent and 4.9 percent respectively, against a closed-canopy reference of 70-85 percent. These figures confirm structurally simplified, low-density forest at both sites.

The two sites tell different stories about how degradation occurred. Save River Delta is heterogeneous: two productive patches within the site show biomass of 20-27 Mg/ha and canopy closure above 30 percent, while the remaining areas are sparse. This mosaic suggests past selective clearing with refugia of mature forest remaining - restoration potential is not foreclosed. Morrumbene is uniformly sparse across all measured areas, with almost no variation in canopy height, biomass, or cover. Uniform stress of this kind points to a systemic driver operating across the whole site. Satellite data are consistent with a freshwater deficit as one possible driver. Upstream river regulation is one plausible cause that warrants hydrological investigation before any attribution is made to specific infrastructure or cross-boundary factors.

### Finding 2: These forests hold a measurable and significant carbon stock

Total carbon stocks at the two study sites, derived from satellite biomass measurements:

- Save River Delta: 71,700 Mg C, equivalent to 263,000 Mg CO2
- Morrumbene Estuary: 8,630 Mg C, equivalent to 31,700 Mg CO2
- Combined total: 80,330 Mg C, equivalent to 294,700 Mg CO2

Estimated annual carbon sequestration at Save River Delta, based on typical tropical mangrove sequestration rates of 4-5 tonnes of carbon per hectare per year, is approximately 55,000 tC per year. At current voluntary carbon market rates of USD 5-10 per tonne of carbon, this translates to a potential USD 275,000-550,000 per year in indicative carbon revenue from Save alone - based on aboveground biomass only, and assuming sequestration rates under restored or intact conditions rather than the current degraded state. This is an indicative estimate, not a contracted revenue projection.

This figure is derived from the following steps: satellite measurements of forest biomass; conversion of biomass to carbon stock using standard factors; multiplication by typical tropical mangrove annual sequestration rates; and application of current voluntary carbon market prices. Each step involves assumptions disclosed in this brief. The figure is an estimate, not a contracted revenue projection.

Note: both study sites are currently functioning at approximately one-fifth of their structural potential; actual sequestration rates under current degraded conditions are likely lower. This figure represents the carbon sequestration potential as these forests recover toward reference condition - it is not an estimate of current annual uptake.

These figures are based on aboveground biomass only. Mangrove soils can hold 50-60 percent of total ecosystem carbon in belowground stocks - and in some systems, soil organic carbon accounts for up to 90 percent of total ecosystem carbon storage in anaerobic sediments. Field surveys planned for 2026 will allow more complete estimates. The numbers presented here are therefore minimum estimates.

Under the Verra VM0033 v2.1 methodology - the international standard used for Africa's largest blue carbon projects, including MozBlue in Zambézia Province - satellite-derived aboveground biomass measurements of the kind provided by NASA GEDI are a recognised input for establishing initial carbon stocks. The 2026 soil coring programme will add the soil organic carbon component. VM0033 permits the use of default soil carbon sequestration values (1.46 tC/ha/yr) as an interim measure, meaning credit issuance can proceed before field soil data is complete. The aboveground biomass baseline established here is therefore sufficient to initiate the registration process without waiting for the full field campaign.

Under current market conditions, VM0033-registered mangrove restoration credits command a significant price premium over standard avoidance credits. This premium reflects both the permanence of blue carbon storage in anaerobic soils and the co-benefits - biodiversity, fisheries nursery function, and coastal protection - that buyers in sectors including aviation, fashion, and consumer goods increasingly require as part of high-integrity nature-based purchases.

The satellite biomass data used here were derived from NASA GEDI LIDAR observations (2024-2025). Mangrove extent data are from the Global Mangrove Watch dataset produced by Aberystwyth University, an internationally recognised standard for national-scale mangrove monitoring. These are not locally produced estimates - they draw on globally validated remote sensing infrastructure.

### Finding 3: A monitoring baseline now exists where none existed before

The 2024-2025 GEDI satellite dataset provides the first standardised, spatially explicit condition account for Mozambique's southern mangroves. This is not a research output in the conventional sense - it is an accounting baseline: a documented starting point against which future change can be measured, budgeted for, and governed. Ecosystem accounts do not just describe nature - they integrate it into the systems governments already use to make decisions. Once a baseline exists, it can anchor budget allocations, inform spatial plans, underpin climate finance applications, and support community land rights claims. The value of the account grows with each subsequent use.

The baseline supports three specific and immediate policy uses:

First, it enables carbon MRV (monitoring, reporting, and verification) for climate finance applications. Both the Green Climate Fund and voluntary carbon markets require a documented baseline before sequestration credits or conservation payments can be issued. That baseline now exists for Save and Morrumbene.

Second, it provides evidence for spatial planning. The identification of productive patches within Save River Delta - and their contrast with degraded surrounding areas - gives planners a basis for distinguishing high-value conservation areas from lower-priority zones. Aquaculture siting decisions can be informed by where forest productivity is highest, rather than where land is most accessible.

Third, it establishes a reference point for NDC and RAMSAR reporting. Mozambique has made commitments to mangrove conservation through its 2021 Nationally Determined Contribution and through RAMSAR wetland designations in Gaza and Inhambane. Until now, those commitments lacked a quantified condition baseline to monitor against. They now have one.

This condition account is precisely the type of georeferenced, standardised dataset that Mozambique's ProAzul Blue Economy Observatory is designed to host and disseminate. The Observatory provides investors with a national map of blue economy activities and GIS-based ecosystem health data - exactly the infrastructure through which sites like Save and Morrumbene can become visible to international climate finance actors. Uploading the Save and Morrumbene account data to the Observatory converts it from a national asset into an internationally visible investment signal.

## WHAT THIS MEANS FOR POLICY

### For the Ministry of Sea, Inland Waterways and Fisheries

Mangroves are nursery habitat for the fish species that sustain artisanal fisheries along Mozambique's southern coast. The structural condition of mangroves - canopy density, root complexity, sediment stability - determines how effectively they perform this nursery function. The finding that both study sites are operating at one-fifth of structural potential is therefore relevant to fisheries productivity, not only to forestry.

Artisanal fishing communities along the Save and Morrumbene coastlines depend on these mangroves directly for subsistence and livelihoods. Any monitoring programme should incorporate community-level participation in data collection and share findings with fishing communities in accessible formats.

At present, there is no baseline measurement of fish recruitment rates in these mangrove systems. Establishing that link - mangrove condition to fish recruitment to landings - is the next step required to quantify the fisheries value of this ecosystem and incorporate it into fisheries management planning. The SEEA EA framework provides the accounting structure for this.

As a preparatory step, the Ministry should commission fish recruitment surveys in Save River Delta to establish whether the observed variation in mangrove structure translates to measurable differences in fish use of those areas. Once that evidence is available, it will provide a defensible basis for incorporating mangrove condition data into aquaculture siting guidance.

### For the Ministry of Environment

The carbon stock baseline - 80,330 Mg C combined across the two sites - is directly relevant to Mozambique's climate finance strategy. The institutional pathway for translating this baseline into registered blue carbon finance is ProAzul and its operational arm ProsperAzul, co-funded by the European Union and Germany. ProAzul is actively mobilising investment in blue economy assets, including mangrove carbon, and functions as the national clearinghouse through which projects of this type are structured, brokered, and brought to international markets.

The governing policy framework for this process is the Blue Economy Development Strategy (EDEA 2024-2033), approved by the Council of Ministers in September 2024. Pillar 3 of the EDEA - Natural Capital, Environment and Circular Economy - governs blue carbon project registration and oversight. The EDEA introduced a transparent fee schedule for carbon market participation: project registration costs the equivalent of 20 national minimum wages; activity licensing 15 minimum wages; monitoring and verification 10 minimum wages; and a 2 percent transaction fee on gross credit value is the primary fiscal instrument for the state. These fees are modest and predictable - they are not a barrier to entry.

The Ministry should initiate the process of registering the Save and Morrumbene carbon stocks under the VM0033 methodology through the ProAzul pathway, with a view to accessing blue carbon finance within the current NDC cycle. The marginal cost of doing so is low: the baseline observations are already collected, and VM0033 permits use of default soil carbon values while the 2026 field campaign is completed.

The Ministry should also flag the Morrumbene finding on freshwater deficit to the relevant water management authorities. If hydrological investigation confirms that upstream water management is contributing to freshwater deficit at Morrumbene, mangrove conservation in Inhambane may depend in part on inter-ministerial and potentially transboundary coordination on river flow management - a finding that warrants early investigation, not conclusion.

### For the Ministry of Land

Community access and use rights in mangrove zones are governed by the Land Law (Law No. 19/1997) through the DUAT framework. Restrictions on conversion of mangrove ecosystems also derive from the Environmental Law (Law No. 20/1997), the Forestry and Wildlife Law (Law No. 10/1999), and the Environmental Impact Assessment Decree (No. 54/2015). Policy recommendations on mangrove land use must engage all three frameworks. This account gives economic content to the "value" of mangrove land - carbon stocks, sequestration potential, and climate finance revenue - that is currently absent from land-use trade-off decisions in mangrove zones.

The data in this brief provides that quantification: each hectare of Save River Delta mangrove holds on average 5.94 Mg C and contributes to sequestration potential of approximately 4-5 tC per year, in addition to its fisheries nursery and coastal protection functions. These values belong in the land-use trade-off analysis.

Mozambique's Carbon Market Regulations (Decree 81/2023) codify a mandatory minimum benefit-sharing arrangement as a legal prerequisite for carbon credit validation: at least 20 percent of gross credit revenue must flow to affected communities, and at least 15 percent to the state. A Benefit-Sharing Agreement (BSA) is required before any project can be validated. The DUAT and customary occupation rights framework already established for Save and Morrumbene communities aligns directly with the BSA requirement - the community rights mapping is the foundation on which the BSA is built.

The MozBlue project in Zambézia Province provides a concrete model: its tripartite benefit-sharing arrangement allocates 33.3 percent of gross revenue to local communities through community fishing councils, exceeding the Decree 81/2023 minimum of 20 percent and delivering over 7,500 direct beneficiaries with 50 percent women. This standard - exceeding the legal minimum and channelling community funds through established governance structures - should be the model for any ProAzul-facilitated carbon project at Save or Morrumbene.

Without the SEEA EA ecosystem baseline, the additionality and permanence claims of any future carbon project at these sites cannot be independently verified. The account is not only a scientific output - it is the legal and evidentiary foundation that makes the BSA calculable and the carbon project registerable.

The Ministry should consider incorporating SEEA EA ecosystem account data into the environmental assessment requirements for new aquaculture concessions in mangrove zones.

### For Parliamentary Committees and Regional Authorities

Gaza and Inhambane provinces have designated mangrove areas under RAMSAR. These international designations carry reporting obligations - and the 2024-2025 satellite baseline is directly usable for those reports. Regional authorities should be aware that the condition indices now available for Save and Morrumbene represent the lowest end of what is reported across the WIO region. Documenting this - and committing to monitoring - positions Mozambique as a transparent and credible partner in regional and international conservation forums.

For parliamentarians, the key figure is the potential carbon market value: USD 275,000-550,000 per year in indicative revenue from Save River Delta alone. This figure is based on aboveground carbon stocks only, assumes restored forest conditions rather than the current degraded state, and is not a contracted revenue projection. It is currently unrealised because no MRV system is in place.

The Parliamentary Committee on Environment and Natural Resources is invited to:

1. Consider an oversight inquiry into the current status of RAMSAR reporting obligations for Gaza and Inhambane designated wetland areas, and whether the 2024-2025 satellite condition baseline has been formally submitted to the RAMSAR Secretariat.
2. Request a written commitment from the Ministry of Environment on the timeline for initiating MRV registration for Save River Delta carbon stocks through the ProAzul institutional pathway.
3. Recommend that the 2027 national budget allocation for environmental monitoring include a specific line for repeat satellite acquisition and field validation at the two study sites.

## WHAT NEEDS TO HAPPEN NEXT

The following actions are concrete and near-term. Each has a clear responsible party and is achievable within the current planning cycle.

**1. Conduct field validation surveys at Save and Morrumbene (Q2-Q3 2026)**
Responsible: Ministry of Environment, in partnership with the University of Eduardo Mondlane and/or University of Toliara (with regional expertise).
The satellite baseline is policy-ready for extent and carbon today. But condition trends - whether these forests are stable, recovering, or degrading - require field ground-truthing. Twelve validation sites (six per location) should be surveyed against the satellite data before the end of 2026. These surveys will also establish seedling recruitment baselines and species composition data, both essential for long-term monitoring. Soil coring for below-ground organic carbon should be integrated into these surveys to supplement the VM0033 default values already available.

**2. Initiate blue carbon MRV registration for Save River Delta through ProAzul (Q2-Q3 2026)**
Responsible: Ministry of Environment, working through ProAzul/ProsperAzul as the institutional pathway, with support from the Climate Finance Unit.
Before initiating registration, the Ministry of Environment should establish that community use rights in the Save and Morrumbene areas have been mapped, and that a Benefit-Sharing Agreement consistent with Decree 81/2023 and DUAT requirements is in place. Carbon credit revenue accruing from community-used mangrove areas cannot be registered without this foundation. The appropriate methodology is Verra VM0033 v2.1 (Tidal Wetland and Seagrass Restoration). The aboveground carbon stock data from the GEDI baseline is sufficient to begin registration; VM0033 default soil carbon values (1.46 tC/ha/yr) can be applied as an interim measure until the 2026 soil coring surveys are complete, allowing credit issuance to begin before full field data is available. Registration fees under the EDEA 2024-2033 fee schedule are modest and predictable. The ProAzul Blue Economy Observatory should be used to make the Save and Morrumbene baseline data visible to potential developers and investors. Critically, the global capacity of independent validation and verification bodies (VVBs) is currently lagging demand for blue carbon project reviews; VVB queue times are a known cause of project delays. Initiating the registration process now - rather than waiting for field validation completion - reduces this risk materially. The MozBlue template, in which developers, government, and communities each receive one-third of gross credit revenue, provides a benefit-sharing model that exceeds Decree 81/2023 minimums and should be the standard for any ProAzul-facilitated project at Save or Morrumbene.

**3. Commission a cross-ministry review of freshwater flows to Morrumbene Estuary (Q2 2026)**
Responsible: Ministry of Environment; Ministry of Public Works and Water Resources; regional authorities in Inhambane.
The uniformity of low condition across all three measured areas of Morrumbene is an early signal of systemic stress. Satellite data are consistent with a freshwater deficit as one possible driver. Upstream river regulation is one plausible cause. A targeted review of seasonal discharge patterns into the Morrumbene river system should be commissioned before the 2026 field surveys so that field teams can test the hypothesis directly. No attribution to specific infrastructure or cross-boundary factors should be made before this hydrological investigation is complete.

**4. Establish a routine satellite monitoring schedule for southern mangroves (Q3 2026)**
Responsible: Ministry of Environment; National Institute for Hydrological and Meteorological Research (INAHM).
The 2024-2025 GEDI observations are a first point in time. They only become a monitoring system when a second observation point is added. A repeat satellite acquisition for Save and Morrumbene should be scheduled for 2027, with data processing resourced within existing environmental monitoring budgets. This is low-cost and high-value: it converts a baseline into a trend.

**5. Integrate ecosystem account data into the Gaza Province aquaculture spatial plan (Q3-Q4 2026)**
Responsible: Ministry of Land; Ministry of Sea, Inland Waterways and Fisheries; Gaza Provincial Directorate of Agriculture and Fisheries.
Gaza Province aquaculture is expanding at 5-10 percent per year. The satellite data now identifies which mangrove patches within Save River Delta have highest productivity and carbon density. This information should be formally incorporated into the provincial aquaculture spatial planning process before new concessions are issued in the Save estuary zone. The Ministry of Land and the Gaza Provincial Directorate should assess whether the EIA Decree (No. 54/2015) provides an appropriate mechanism - for example, a condition attached to new concession EIA approvals - to ensure this spatial information is considered before further conversion of high-condition mangrove areas. MSMIF should participate in that assessment. This approach uses the existing legal framework rather than creating a parallel process.

## HOW THIS FITS EXISTING COMMITMENTS

This work does not create new obligations. It provides the evidence base to deliver on commitments Mozambique has already made. More broadly, it demonstrates what ecosystem accounting makes possible: natural assets that were previously invisible in budget decisions and policy negotiations are now quantified, mapped, and attributable to specific administrative areas. That shift - from invisible to measurable - is the foundational contribution of a SEEA EA account, and it compounds over time as successive accounts build a longitudinal record of how Mozambique's ecosystems are changing.

**Nationally Determined Contribution (2021):** Mozambique's NDC includes explicit pledges on mangrove conservation for climate mitigation and coastal resilience. The SEEA EA account presented here provides the quantified baseline - extent, carbon stocks, and condition indices - against which NDC progress can be reported. Without this baseline, the mangrove pledges in the NDC are unverifiable. With it, they are monitorable and credible to international climate finance institutions.

**RAMSAR Wetland Designations (Gaza and Inhambane):** RAMSAR designations for the Save and Morrumbene estuary areas commit Mozambique to reporting on wetland condition and management effectiveness. The condition indices developed here (0.17-0.19 on a 0-1 scale) provide a quantified basis for those reports - and they document that the designated areas are under significant structural stress, which strengthens the case for conservation investment rather than weakening it.

**Blue Economy Development Strategy (EDEA 2024-2033):** Approved by the Council of Ministers in September 2024, the EDEA commits Mozambique to treating its marine and coastal ecosystems as measurable natural capital and to operationalising blue carbon finance through clear institutional and fiscal frameworks. Pillar 3 - Natural Capital, Environment and Circular Economy - directly governs the registration, monitoring, and revenue-sharing arrangements for carbon market participation. This account operationalises that commitment for mangroves: extent is mapped, carbon stocks are quantified, degradation is documented, and indicative service values are estimated. The EDEA's transparent fee schedule for carbon project registration makes the institutional pathway predictable and accessible.

**Carbon Market Regulations (Decree 81/2023):** Mozambique's Carbon Market Regulations codify benefit-sharing as a legal prerequisite for credit validation. The SEEA EA account establishes the ecosystem baseline against which those benefits are calculated - without it, the additionality and permanence claims of any future carbon project at these sites cannot be independently verified. The account is therefore not only a scientific contribution but a legal enabler of the carbon rights framework Decree 81/2023 establishes.

**General Law on Fisheries (Law No. 3/1990, updated 2017):** The Fisheries Law mandates sustainable management of fish resources, including the habitat systems that support them. Mangroves are a critical fisheries habitat. This account establishes the condition baseline for that habitat. Future accounts linking mangrove condition to fish recruitment rates will provide the direct evidence for fisheries-based management decisions on mangrove protection.

**Community and land rights framework:** Community customary occupation rights and formal DUAT allocations under the Land Law (Law No. 19/1997), together with the Environmental Law (Law No. 20/1997), the Forestry and Wildlife Law (Law No. 10/1999), and the EIA Decree (No. 54/2015), collectively govern mangrove land use and conversion. This account gives economic content to the value of mangrove land - carbon stocks, sequestration potential, and climate finance revenue - that is currently absent from land-use trade-off decisions, and provides a foundation for integrating ecosystem values into that full legal framework.

## A NOTE ON WHAT WE DO NOT YET KNOW

The evidence presented in this brief is sufficient to support all five recommended actions. The following gaps affect the precision of some estimates but do not change the core findings.

**Gaps that will refine precision but do not affect the recommended actions:**

- Soil and belowground carbon: belowground stocks have not yet been measured at field level. This affects the carbon revenue estimates, which should be treated as minimum figures, but does not affect the case for initiating MRV registration. VM0033 default soil carbon values can be applied as an interim measure. The 2026 soil coring programme will refine these estimates and reduce uncertainty in credit calculations.
- Coastal protection value: the economic value of storm buffering and erosion control provided by Save and Morrumbene mangroves has not yet been quantified. This affects the completeness of the economic picture but does not alter the recommended actions on fisheries or carbon.
- Sequestration rates under degraded versus restored conditions: current revenue estimates apply typical intact-forest rates to forests that are operating at one-fifth of structural potential. Actual sequestration under current conditions is likely lower. This affects the precision of the revenue figure but not the case for establishing a monitoring system.

**Gaps that require new data before specific policy steps can be taken:**

- Fish recruitment rates: the relationship between mangrove structural condition and fish use of these areas has not yet been measured at these sites. This information must be gathered before fisheries-based aquaculture siting guidance can be issued with confidence. This is why Action 1 prioritises field surveys, and why the Ministry of Sea recommendation frames recruitment surveys as a preparatory step rather than an immediate management rule.

The satellite baseline is a snapshot, not a trend. We know the condition of these forests in 2024-2025. We do not yet know whether they are stable, improving, or continuing to degrade. The planned 2026 field surveys will begin to answer that question, and a repeat satellite acquisition in 2027 will establish a two-point trend for the first time.

Despite these gaps, the data in hand are sufficient for the policy actions recommended above. Good policy does not require complete information - it requires the best available evidence and honest disclosure of what remains uncertain. And the ecosystem account framework is designed precisely for this: each iteration of the account fills a gap, tightens an estimate, and expands the range of policy decisions it can support. The account tabled here is a beginning, not an endpoint.

With ProAzul mobilising nearly USD 6 billion in blue economy investment and fewer than 15 blue carbon projects globally capable of issuing VM0033-compliant credits, the supply-demand gap in high-integrity mangrove carbon creates an extraordinary window of opportunity. The SEEA EA account for Save and Morrumbene is the first infrastructure investment required to enter that market. The policy question is not whether to act, but how quickly the institutional foundation can be established.
