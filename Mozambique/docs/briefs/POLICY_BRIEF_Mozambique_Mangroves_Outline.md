## PROJECT BRIEFING

This document outlines the structure and policy communication strategy for Mozambique's mangrove ecosystem account, following the SEEA EA (System of Environmental-Economic Accounting — Ecosystem Accounting) framework. This brief is in the planning phase.

**Current Status:**
- ✅ Ecosystem extent: Mapped (Sentinel-2 mangrove classification; pending field validation)
- ⏳ Biotic condition (tree density, health, species composition): Field surveys planned Q2–Q4 2026
- ⏳ Services valuation: Depends on condition data + external market data (fish landings, carbon stocks, coastal protection)
- ⏳ Policy engagement: Stakeholder consultation planned for Q1 2026

**Key Features of This Account:**
- Focuses on **southern Mozambique mangrove forests** (Gaza + Inhambane provinces) as pilot

- Existing governance anchors: SANParks-managed areas, RAMSAR wetland designations
- Policy entry points: Climate adaptation funding (GCF, Adaptation Fund); blue economy strategy; NDC climate commitments

## PAGE 1: SITUATION + DATA SNAPSHOT

### Headline Title

**Measuring Mozambique's Mangroves: A Baseline for Natural Capital & Climate Action**

*Rationale:* Data now available from 2024–2025 GEDI satellite observations, establishing extent and carbon baseline for policy-ready ecosystem accounting.

---

### Situation Statement

Mozambique's mangrove forests are critical natural capital: they support artisanal fisheries, sequester carbon for climate mitigation, and protect coastal communities from cyclones and erosion. Yet policy decisions on mangrove conservation, aquaculture expansion, and coastal development proceed without quantified ecosystem baselines.

**The gap:** Mozambique monitors national extent of mangroves at ~407,000 hectares (GMW 2020 global dataset), but lacks institutionalized **condition monitoring** (forest health, productivity, carbon stocks). Two key study sites—Save River Delta (~12,085 ha, Gaza Province) and Morrumbene Estuary (~1,677 ha, Inhambane Province)—are now instrumented with 2024–2025 satellite data (GEDI LIDAR) that reveal forest structure and biomass density. These sites represent the southernmost mangrove ecosystems in Mozambique, facing acute pressures from aquaculture expansion, coastal erosion, and freshwater stress from upstream dam operations.

**The opportunity:** Ecosystem accounting based on satellite observations establishes a **policy-ready baseline**—one that can track progress toward Mozambique's NDC mangrove conservation pledges, inform spatial planning for aquaculture vs. fisheries trade-offs, quantify climate finance eligibility (carbon markets, GCF), and guide RAMSAR wetland management effectiveness.

---

### The Data: 3 Key Findings from GEDI Satellite Observations (2024–2025)

**Finding 1: Two Contrasting Mangrove Systems**

**Save River Delta** (12,085 ha):
- **Mean canopy height:** 5.07 m | **Aboveground biomass:** 12.63 Mg/ha | **Canopy cover:** 8.8%
- **Carbon stock:** 5.94 Mg C/ha (baseline: ~71,700 Mg C total across site)
- **Spatial heterogeneity:** Assets range from 9–27 Mg/ha biomass. Two standout patches (Assets 3 & 7) show mature forest structure (20–27 Mg/ha, 30–43% canopy closure); remainder are sparse woodlands. Suggests patchwork of productive refugia + degraded areas.

**Morrumbene Estuary** (1,677 ha):
- **Mean canopy height:** 5.27 m | **Aboveground biomass:** 10.95 Mg/ha | **Canopy cover:** 4.9%
- **Carbon stock:** 5.15 Mg C/ha (baseline: ~8,630 Mg C total across site)
- **Spatial homogeneity:** Three assets nearly identical (10–11 Mg/ha biomass, 4.6–5.1% canopy cover). Suggests uniformly sparse woodland—likely stressed by salinity or upstream dam regulation.

**Policy implication:** Save Estuary is larger and more carbon-rich, but heterogeneous (mosaic of productive + degraded patches amenable to targeted restoration). Morrumbene is smaller and uniformly stressed (indicates systemic pressure—possibly freshwater deficit from Limpopo dam).

---

**Finding 2: Carbon Baseline for Climate Finance**

**Aggregated carbon inventory (2020 GMW extent):**
- **Save Estuary:** ~71,700 Mg C (263,000 Mg CO₂ equiv)
- **Morrumbene Estuary:** ~8,630 Mg C (31,700 Mg CO₂ equiv)
- **Combined study sites:** ~80,300 Mg C

**Annual sequestration (estimated):** ~4–5 tC/ha/yr (mangrove typical range). At Save's 12,085 ha: ~55,000 tC/yr carbon sequestration = potential USD 275,000–550,000/year in carbon market value (at USD 5–10/tC voluntary carbon rates).

**Policy implication:** Baseline enables Mozambique to access blue carbon funding (GCF, Adaptation Fund, voluntary carbon markets). Tracking changes in extent & biomass post-2026 enables "carbon MRV" (monitoring, reporting, verification) for climate projects.

---

**Finding 3: Data Quality & Confidence**

**GEDI satellite coverage:**
- **Save Estuary:** 84 GEDI footprints (sparse; uneven by asset; uncertainty ±16 Mg/ha)
- **Morrumbene Estuary:** 195 GEDI footprints (dense coverage; uniform confidence)

**Interpretation:** Morrumbene biomass estimates are more reliable for policy use. Save Estuary's heterogeneity is real but not all patches are equally well-measured. Field validation surveys (planned Q2–Q3 2026) will ground-truth satellite estimates and identify species composition + recruitment status.

**Policy implication:** Baseline is **policy-ready today** for extent + carbon reporting. **Condition trends** (growth/decline) require field validation post-2026.

---

### CALLOUT BOX: Data Translates to Policy Action

**From Satellite Observation to Decision Support**

Save Estuary and Morrumbene mangroves represent ~80,000 Mg C locked in biomass—a natural climate asset worth USD 400,000–800,000 in voluntary carbon market value (at USD 5–10/tC). This carbon inventory was invisible to Mozambique's fiscal planners 6 months ago. Today, it is **quantified and policy-ready**.

The 2026 field validation will add the missing piece: **condition trends**. Is mangrove biomass stable, growing, or declining? Which patches are being lost to aquaculture? Where is recruitment failing? These questions cannot be answered by satellite alone. But combined with 2026 ground-truthing, this baseline becomes a **living monitoring system** that tracks progress toward Mozambique's NDC mangrove restoration pledges and enables evidence-based trade-off decisions on aquaculture siting, coastal protection investment, and climate finance allocation.

---

### Policy Gap Identified

**Baseline now available; systematic monitoring remains absent.**

The 2024–2025 GEDI baseline fills a critical data gap:
- **Extent & carbon:** Save (12,085 ha, 71,700 Mg C) and Morrumbene (1,677 ha, 8,630 Mg C) are now quantified and mapped
- **Spatial heterogeneity:** Save's mosaic of productive + degraded patches identified; Morrumbene's systemic stress documented
- **Climate finance eligibility:** Carbon baseline enables access to GCF, Adaptation Fund, and carbon market funding

**What remains missing:**
1. **Institutionalized annual monitoring:** No recurring system for MSMIF/Environment to track biomass, extent, or carbon changes year-to-year
2. **Condition trends:** 2026 baseline is a snapshot; field validation (Q2–Q3 2026) needed to establish whether mangroves are recovering, stable, or degrading
3. **Fisheries linkage:** Mangrove biomass/structure measured, but fish recruitment rates and economic landings value still unmeasured
4. **Coastal protection value:** Mangrove extent protects villages from cyclones; flood protection asset value not quantified in budget decisions
5. **Cross-ministry coordination:** MSMIF (fisheries), Environment, and Land ministries each monitor mangroves independently; no shared condition metric for integrated coastal zone management

**This account provides the foundation—field validation + policy engagement will operationalize it.**

### Why This Matters: Local Context

Mozambique's mangrove ecosystems—especially the Save and Morrumbene estuaries in Gaza and Inhambane provinces—are critical to southern Mozambique's food security and climate resilience. Artisanal fishing communities in these regions depend directly on mangrove-supported fish recruitment; coastal villages rely on mangrove forests for cyclone protection; and Mozambique's international climate commitments (NDC) depend on maintaining mangrove extent and carbon stocks.

**Documented threats (2020–2026):**
- **Aquaculture expansion:** Gaza Province aquaculture footprint growing 5–10%/year; converting mangrove habitat
- **Coastal erosion:** Save and Morrumbene estuaries affected by sea-level rise and increased storm intensity
- **Freshwater stress:** Limpopo River dam operations alter freshwater flow to Save Delta, increasing salinity stress and tree mortality
- **Timber harvesting:** Informal mangrove cutting for charcoal and construction materials ongoing

**Why the baseline matters now:**
This 2024–2025 GEDI account provides evidence that was invisible to policymakers 6 months ago. Fisheries ministry can now quantify the mangrove-fisheries link. Environment ministry can track progress on NDC pledges. Ministry of Land has data to guide spatial planning decisions. Coastal communities have evidence to defend livelihood interests in mangrove protection.

The baseline is not prescriptive—it does not say "do X or Y." It is a tool that enables stakeholders (government, communities, donors, developers) to ground their concerns and priorities in shared evidence. That shared evidence is what transforms policy conversations from anecdotal (fishers say catches are declining) to quantitative (mangrove carbon stock baseline established; field validation in 2026 will show whether declining).

### What Data Could Enable (Once Collected in 2026)

**For Fisheries Ministry & Coastal Zone Management:**
- Once fish recruitment density in mangrove habitat is measured, officials can quantify the fisheries production supported by mangrove nursery habitat
- Suggest: Use baseline data to identify which mangrove areas are most productive for fisheries; prioritize mangrove protection in high-productivity zones
- Mangrove extent + condition can guide aquaculture siting decisions (avoid highest-productivity nursery habitat)

**For Climate Finance & Carbon Markets:**
- Mangrove carbon stocks (aboveground + belowground biomass) are a quantifiable climate mitigation asset
- Suggest: Use baseline carbon inventory to access GCF or carbon market funding; track carbon sequestration as a climate adaptation service
- Baseline enables "carbon MRV" (monitoring, reporting, verification) for international climate commitments

**For Coastal Protection & Disaster Risk Reduction:**
- Mangrove extent + health indicate flood/cyclone protection capacity for coastal communities
- Suggest: Use condition data in coastal spatial planning; quantify avoided flood damages from mangrove protection vs. mangrove loss
- Economic value of "ecosystem-based adaptation" (mangrove protection as natural disaster insurance) can justify conservation investment

**For Protected Area Management & RAMSAR Reporting:**
- Mangrove condition baseline for RAMSAR-designated wetlands enables effectiveness monitoring
- Suggest: Compare condition inside vs. outside designated areas; use data for RAMSAR reports to international convention
- Demonstrates to donors that Mozambique is delivering on wetland conservation commitments

**For Community Livelihood Negotiation:**
- Fish recruitment + mangrove tree density data ground community concerns about livelihood impacts from mangrove loss
- Suggest: Share data with fishing communities and mangrove resource users; use evidence in negotiations with government and private developers
- Enables communities to quantify "losses" from mangrove conversion and negotiate compensation or alternative livelihood support

**For Land Rights & Community-Based Resource Management:**
- Mangrove extent + condition data support community claims to traditional mangrove harvesting areas
- Suggest: Integrate ecosystem account data into land use planning processes; use evidence to defend community access rights in disputes with developers

### Suggestions to Consider

Once baseline condition is established:

1. **Share findings openly:** Publish preliminary results (even before full valuation); transparency builds stakeholder confidence and enables dialogue with fishing communities, industry, government, and NGOs

2. **Compare inside vs. outside protected areas:** If RAMSAR-designated mangrove areas show better condition than adjacent unprotected areas, this is evidence for protection effectiveness—useful for budget arguments and donor reports

3. **Identify productivity hotspots:** Some mangrove patches may show higher fish recruitment, higher tree density, or higher carbon stocks than others. These become priority areas for protection, co-management, or restoration

4. **Quantify livelihood linkages:** Use fish recruitment data + fishing household surveys to estimate "number of jobs supported per hectare of mangrove"—powerful metric for policy debates

5. **Link to climate finance:** Frame mangrove protection as climate adaptation + mitigation. Baseline carbon stocks and coastal protection capacity make mangroves eligible for GCF and Adaptation Fund funding

6. **Use for stakeholder negotiation:** Present data to fishing communities, mangrove timber users, aquaculture developers, and government. Let each group see the mangrove value most relevant to their interests

7. **Consider follow-on work:** Identify which questions the account answered well, and which remain. This sets agenda for next-phase research (e.g., livelihood modeling, carbon MRV for carbon markets, restoration potential assessment)
