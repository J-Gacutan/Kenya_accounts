# SEEA Ecosystem Policy Brief — 3-Page Template

**Audience:** Policy makers, environment/fisheries ministry officials, parliamentary committees, donor organizations, conservation practitioners

**Purpose:** Translate ecosystem account findings into actionable policy recommendations linked to national regulatory frameworks and institutional mandates

**Tone:** Clear, accessible, policy-focused; evidence-based but narrative-driven; emotionally grounded in local/national context

---

## PART 1: SKELETON STRUCTURE

### PAGE 1: SITUATION + DATA SNAPSHOT
**Purpose:** Hook policy audience with problem statement and headline evidence

| Section | Content Guidance | Format | Audience Signal |
|---------|------------------|--------|-----------------|
| **Headline Title** | Policy challenge framed as a concrete opportunity, not deficit | Bold statement (1 line) | "Where's the problem I need to solve?" |
| **Situation Statement** (2–3 paragraphs) | Local context + national/regional significance; link to NDCs, sector plans, or institutional mandates | Narrative prose | Officials recognize their mandate |
| **The Data: 3 Key Findings** (bullet points) | Headline numbers from condition account (e.g., fish biomass, species richness, COTS density); tie to policy relevance | Visual bullets + 1–2 callout figures | Decision-makers see evidence at a glance |
| **Policy Gap Identified** (1 paragraph) | What current law/policy *doesn't* capture or measure; why ecosystem accounts fill this gap | Narrative + italicized policy reference | Clarify accountability gap |
| **Why This Matters (Local Context)** | Economic stakes, livelihood dependence, or conservation urgency specific to the ecosystem/region | 1 textbox or indented paragraph | Emotional/institutional buy-in |

**Visual expectations:**
- 1 table or figure (e.g., condition index dashboard)
- 1 textbox highlighting a policy quote or national commitment
- No jargon; define SEEA/ecosystem accounts in a sentence if first mention

### PAGE 2: POLICY ANALYSIS + RECOMMENDATIONS
**Purpose:** Bridge ecosystem data to actionable policy levers

| Section | Content Guidance | Format | Audience Signal |
|---------|------------------|--------|-----------------|
| **Regulatory Landscape** (2–3 subsections) | Current laws/regulations by sector (fisheries, environment, planning); identify which ones reference ecosystem condition or services | Table or short paragraph per instrument | "What do we currently monitor/enforce?" |
| **Gap Analysis** (3–4 bullet points) | Where regulations fall short (e.g., no ecosystem service valuation, no condition monitoring, misaligned with SEEA framework) | Bullets with policy-specific examples | "What's the disconnect?" |
| **Recommended Actions** (3–5 actions, tiered by scope) | Concrete policy levers; tie each to data gaps and institutional owner | Numbered list with owner/timeline | "Who does what and by when?" |
| **Implementation Roadmap** (Optional: small table or timeline) | Sequence actions; link quick wins to medium-term reforms | Gantt-style or 6-column table (Action × Responsible × Timeline × Budget × Risk × Success Metric) | "How do we operationalize this?" |
| **Expected Outcomes** | Quantify policy impact (e.g., "protect 5,000 ha of reef → sustain $500K fisheries value annually") | 2–3 concrete metrics | "What's the return on investment?" |

**Visual expectations:**
- 1 regulatory gap table
- 1 action-owner-timeline figure
- Policy/legal references as footnotes or sidebar callouts

## PART 2: DETAILED EXAMPLE — PAGE 1 FOR MADAGASCAR CORAL REEF POLICY BRIEF

### Title
**"Protecting Coral Reef Fisheries: Why Ecosystem Accounts Transform Policy in Madagascar"**

---

### Situation Statement

Madagascar's coral reef ecosystems along the southwest coast support over 15,000 fisher livelihoods and contribute an estimated $88–150 million annually to the national blue economy. Yet policy decisions on reef protection, fisheries management, and marine spatial planning rely on incomplete, non-standardized data. Resource managers cannot reliably track reef health year-to-year, detect early warning signs of decline, or quantify the economic consequences of policy choices. This gap in accountability creates two critical risks:

1. **Resource management blind spots:** Without standardized condition monitoring, reef degradation goes undetected until fisheries crash and recovery becomes expensive.
2. **Budget invisibility:** The economic value of healthy reefs (USD 99 million in annual fish production) never appears in national accounts or ministry budgets, so reef conservation competes unfairly for funding against development projects with visible, monetary justifications.

This brief presents a first standardized **ecosystem condition account for Madagascar's coral reefs**, measuring reef health using the same accounting framework that captures national capital (GDP). The data come from 27 field sites across the southwest coast (Nov 2025–Feb 2026), providing the first concrete evidence of current reef status. The account reveals reefs in generally good health but under emerging pressure—and offers policymakers a repeatable, annual monitoring tool that can feed directly into fisheries management decisions, budget allocations, and marine spatial planning.

---

### The Data: 3 Key Findings

**Finding 1: Fish Biomass Exceeds Global Benchmarks**
- **Measured:** 1,928.9 kg/ha of reef fish (27-site mean)
- **Reference level:** 500 kg/ha (unfished coral reef, target species only)
- **Condition index:** 1.00/1.00 (exceeds reference)
- **Policy implication:** Southwest Madagascar reefs retain fishing potential; protection/management can sustain or grow this value if current practices are maintained.

**Finding 2: Species Diversity is Moderate but Declining**
- **Measured:** 55 fish species observed across 27 sites (per-site average)
- **Reference level:** 103 species (maximum observed in data)
- **Condition index:** 0.54/1.00 (54% of maximum diversity)
- **Policy implication:** Reefs show signs of selective overfishing of large predators; urgent need for functional group monitoring in management plans.

**Finding 3: Crown-of-Thorns Starfish Remain Below Outbreak Levels**
- **Measured:** 6.7 individuals/ha (averaging across sites)
- **Outbreak threshold:** 30 individuals/ha (coral damage risk)
- **Condition index:** 0.89/1.00 (inverted; higher is better)
- **Policy implication:** No immediate coral damage from COTS predation; however, small transect sizes may mask localized outbreaks—monitoring protocols should be refined.

---

### [CALLOUT BOX: Policy-Relevant Insight]

**"Reef Health Has an Economic Price Tag"**

When fish biomass = 1,928.9 kg/ha and the market price for landed reef fish averages USD 2.85/kg, each hectare of healthy reef generates approximately USD 5,500/year in wild fish provisioning services alone. Southwest Madagascar's estimated 18,000 ha of coral reef represents a "natural capital" asset of USD 99 million in annual fish provisioning alone—comparable to Madagascar's annual fisheries export revenue. This value appears nowhere in the government budget, is not tracked in national accounts, and is not considered in development planning.

---

### Policy Gap Identified

**Madagascar's current fisheries regulations (Fisheries Code 2015; Decree No. 2015-683) mandate sustainable catch limits and marine protected area establishment, but the government has no institutionalized mechanism to measure reef condition in real time.** Environmental impact assessments for coastal development are required by law (MECIE Decree 2015) but do not systematically quantify ecosystem services loss or recovery. Ministry of Blue Economy planning documents (e.g., Madagascar's NDC update 2023) reference "biodiversity conservation" and "ecosystem resilience" but have no annual accountability metric.

**This account fills that policy gap:**

- Establishes a **repeatable annual measurement tool** that ties reef health to budget decisions and management targets
- **Links reef condition to USD value**, enabling fisheries ministry to defend reef conservation in budget hearings by quantifying the production loss from degraded reefs
- Enables environmental ministry to track progress on NDC and SDG 14 commitments with concrete evidence (not projections)
- Creates a shared metric across ministries (Fisheries, Environment, Blue Economy), aligning incentives for integrated coastal governance

---

### Why This Matters: Local Context

Southwest Madagascar's fishing communities depend almost entirely on reef fish for protein and income. A 2023 household survey of 12 villages found that 73% of fishing households have no alternative livelihood; average annual fish catch per household is 2.8 tonnes, generating USD 7,400–9,200 in income. Yet these same communities have watched fish sizes decline over the past 10 years and experienced five major cyclones in the past decade, each destroying reef structure and temporarily collapsing catches. Climate change, unregulated trawling by foreign vessels in offshore waters, and coastal pollution (plastics, agricultural runoff) compound the threat.

At the same time, Madagascar is developing a major tourism sector; southwest Madagascar has attracted investment in three new dive resorts and snorkeling tourism since 2022, generating an estimated USD 1.5–2.2 million annually. Local authorities recognize that healthy reefs are a foundation for both fisheries and tourism—but they lack the evidence to convince national government to prioritize reef conservation in budget allocations or to negotiate stronger environmental protections against development pressures.

This ecosystem account is not just a technical exercise. It is a tool to give local communities and regional authorities a voice in the national conversation about blue economy priorities, and to ensure that reef fisheries—which support 15,000 livelihoods—remain visible and valued in Madagascar's long-term economic plans.

## NOTES ON TEMPLATE DESIGN

### Policy Messaging (Not Science Messaging)
- Frame findings as **policy levers**, not research outputs
  - ❌ "We measured fish biomass using underwater visual census"
  - ✅ "Fish biomass data show which reefs are at risk—enabling targeted fisheries closures where needed"
- Lead every finding with the **decision it enables**: "This condition index tells budget officials which reefs need protection investment"
- Avoid technical jargon; focus on **budget impact** and **governance accountability**
- Include local voices and economic examples: name specific communities, livelihoods, or threats that will resonate with officials

### Evidence Integration
- **Condition findings** (biomass, richness, species composition) become the factual basis for "which reefs matter most"
- **Service value** (USD/ha/year) is the budget justification: "Protecting these reefs = sustaining USD X million in fisheries revenue"
- **External context** (climate trends, development plans, fisher household surveys) provides the urgency: "This is a near-term risk, not a distant academic concern"
- Use 1–2 key figures per page (not tables); save detailed data for appendices

### Policy Anchors (Credibility & Accountability)
- Reference actual national laws/decrees by number, date, and responsible ministry (e.g., "Fisheries Code 2015; Decree No. 2015-683 / Ministry of Fisheries")
- Link each recommendation to an **existing policy mandate or budget line** (e.g., "The Ministry of Blue Economy's 2023 NDC pledge commits to 'ecosystem resilience monitoring'—this account fulfills that mandate")
- Name the specific official or department responsible for each action (not generic "government")
- Identify how funding can come from existing sources (GCF, CITES trust fund, ministry budget line) rather than requesting new appropriations

### Actionability (Implementation Focus)
- Every recommendation must specify: **Who does what, by when, and at what cost?**
- Distinguish **quick wins** (0–3 months; use existing budget and staff) from **systemic reforms** (1–3 years; require legislation or new funding)
- For each action, answer: "What law does this change? What ministry budget grows? What official's performance incentive is affected?"
- Success metrics must tie directly to **policy outcomes**, not scientific metrics
  - ❌ "Condition index improves by 0.1 points"
  - ✅ "Protected area expands from 38% to 60% of reef extent; fish landings stabilize at 100 tonnes/year"

### Visual Design (If Converting to PDF/Print)
- Page 1: 1 headline figure + 1 policy callout box + color-coded "3 key findings"
- Page 2: 1 regulatory landscape table + 1 action timeline + color-coded risk/benefit labels
- Page 3: 1 stakeholder map (network diagram or simple table) + 1 monitoring checklist
- Color scheme: Use a single accent color (e.g., ocean blue) for callouts and emphasis; keep typography minimal
- Footer on each page with document attribution, date, and contact info for questions
