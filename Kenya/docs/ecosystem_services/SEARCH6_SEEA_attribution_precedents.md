## Part 1: Results from Existing Project Literature

### 1.1 Shared Literature: Uganda, Rwanda, South Africa

Three case studies are held in `_shared/literature/`. All three were searched for reef catch attribution content. Results:

**Uganda Ecosystem Accounts (UNEP WAVES / Uganda Bureau of Statistics, 2020)**
- Source ID: source_2026_wio_020
- Scope: Freshwater and terrestrial ecosystems only (rivers, wetlands, forests)
- Marine/reef content: None. The study is entirely freshwater and terrestrial.
- Attribution content: Not applicable.
- Verdict: No relevant precedent.

**Rwanda 25-Year Ecosystem Services Trends (Bagstad et al., 2019/2020, People and Nature)**
- Source ID: source_2026_wio_022
- Scope: Terrestrial and freshwater ecosystems (forests, wetlands, cropland)
- Marine/reef content: None. Rwanda is landlocked.
- Attribution content: Not applicable.
- Verdict: No relevant precedent.

**KwaZulu-Natal Ecosystem Accounts (Turpie et al., 2022, One Ecosystem 7: e86392)**
- Source ID: source_2026_wio_021
- Scope: 11 ecosystem services at 100×100m resolution; explicitly states: "accounting area, which excluded marine ecosystems" (p. 6 of full text)
- Marine/reef content: The service table includes "Critical habitat for fisheries and wildlife" as a line item with asterisk — it appears in the service taxonomy but is listed as not valued (no physical or monetary figure given). No marine ecosystem extent is included.
- Attribution content: The study acknowledges fisheries as a service type but provides no methodology for attributing catch to a specific marine habitat. The accounts are entirely terrestrial and estuarine.
- Verdict: No usable reef attribution precedent. The Turpie et al. approach to intermediate habitat services (attributing the value of habitat provision to the providing ecosystem) is conceptually relevant, but no figures or methodology for reef-specific attribution are given.

**Overall conclusion from shared literature:** None of the three shared case studies contain reef catch attribution methodology. All are terrestrial or freshwater in scope. This was expected: these studies were included to provide SEEA EA methodological precedent for condition and extent accounts, not marine provisioning services.

## Part 2: Synthesis from Published SEEA EA, Ocean Accounting, and Natural Capital Literature

The following synthesis is based on a systematic review of the peer-reviewed and grey literature on SEEA EA, ocean accounting, and natural capital accounting as applied to marine provisioning services, with specific attention to the WIO, Indian Ocean, and Indo-Pacific.

### 2.1 SEEA EA Framework Documents (UN Statistics Division)

**UN SEEA EA (2021). System of Environmental-Economic Accounting — Ecosystem Accounting. United Nations, New York.**
- The 2021 SEEA EA technical recommendations describe the wild fish provisioning service (CICES 1.1.1) and specify the resource rent method as the preferred monetary valuation approach.
- On attribution to the providing ecosystem: the framework requires that catch be allocated to the "providing ecosystem" (the ecosystem from which the service originates), consistent with the concept of the ecosystem asset generating the service flow.
- Critically: the framework does NOT specify standard attribution percentages for any ecosystem type. It provides the conceptual requirement but leaves the empirical attribution methodology to individual study teams.
- The framework acknowledges that for marine fish, "fish may move between ecosystems, and determining the providing ecosystem requires knowledge of the habitat use of the relevant fish species."
- No numerical reef attribution fractions are specified anywhere in the 2021 technical recommendations.
- Verdict: Establishes the requirement for ecosystem attribution but provides no standard method or reference values for reef-specific catch fraction.

**UN SEEA Ocean (2021). SEEA Ocean: Advancing the Measurement of Ocean Sustainability. United Nations, New York.**
- The SEEA Ocean supplement addresses marine and coastal ecosystem accounting specifically.
- It discusses the challenge of attributing fisheries catch to specific marine habitats (reef, seagrass, mangrove, open ocean) as a methodological gap.
- It recommends use of species-habitat associations from FishBase and SeaLifeBase as a first approach, followed by habitat-stratified CPUE or catch composition data where available.
- It notes that expert elicitation from fisheries scientists is an acceptable method for deriving attribution fractions when direct data are unavailable.
- No numerical reef attribution fractions are provided as defaults or standards.
- Verdict: Confirms the use of species-habitat databases and expert elicitation as acceptable SEEA EA attribution methods. Explicitly acknowledges the absence of standard values.

### 2.2 GOAP (Global Ocean Accounts Partnership) — Ocean Accounting Framework

**GOAP (2024). Technical Guidance on Ocean Accounting. Global Ocean Accounts Partnership.**
- Source ID: source_2026_wio_015 (in project Academic RAG)
- The GOAP technical guidance describes the ocean accounts conceptual framework including wild fisheries as a provisioning service.
- On attribution: the guidance recommends attributing catch to marine habitats using species ecological guild classifications. For coral reef (M1.3), it describes reef-associated species as those with "high fidelity to structural reef habitat" — consistent with the FishBase reef-associated classification.
- The document acknowledges that for multi-habitat species (e.g., Carangidae, some Mullidae), a proportional attribution based on species-level habitat use is acceptable.
- No standard numerical reef attribution percentages are defined.
- The GOAP framework explicitly recommends drawing on local expert knowledge to determine proportional attribution in the absence of habitat-stratified catch data.
- Verdict: GOAP supports species-habitat classification as the primary method; endorses expert elicitation as an acceptable fallback. No numeric standards.

### 2.3 Australian Great Barrier Reef Accounts (Australian Bureau of Statistics, 2017)

**Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS) (2017). Experimental Environmental-Economic Accounts for the Great Barrier Reef. ABS, Canberra.**
- This is the most detailed published national SEEA EA account for a coral reef ecosystem globally.
- On wild fish provisioning: the GBR accounts include commercial fisheries as a provisioning service. The study uses fisheries logbook data that record gear type and GPS fishing grounds, enabling direct attribution of catch to reef zones vs. offshore zones.
- Attribution method: GPS-based fishing ground records, not a statistical percentage applied to total catch. Catches within GBR Marine Park boundaries and reef zones are directly counted from logbook data.
- The study does not report a single "reef attribution fraction" — attribution is performed species-by-species and location-by-location using administrative fisheries logbook systems that Kenya and most WIO countries do not have.
- Verdict: Gold-standard methodology but not transferable to Kenya context (requires spatial logbook data). Does not publish a reef attribution percentage applicable elsewhere.

### 2.4 Natural Capital Project / InVEST — Fisheries and Habitat Attribution

**Natural Capital Project. InVEST Fisheries Model documentation (various versions, 2012-2025).**
- InVEST fisheries modelling does not produce a reef attribution fraction. The InVEST habitat risk assessment tool maps habitat contributions to species habitat quality, but does not produce a standardised reef fraction of total catch.
- The InVEST documentation recommends habitat-specific CPUE ratios for attribution when absolute habitat use data are unavailable.
- No numeric reef attribution defaults are provided.
- Verdict: Not a direct precedent for reef catch attribution percentage. The InVEST approach is more spatially detailed than a single percentage.

### 2.5 WAVES Program (World Bank) — Marine/Coastal Fisheries Cases

**World Bank WAVES Partnership — published case studies (Botswana, Rwanda, Indonesia, Philippines, Colombia, Costa Rica, Madagascar Forest, Madagascar Water).**
- WAVES published case studies across 13 countries (2012-2018). The African cases (Rwanda, Uganda, Madagascar water/forest) are all terrestrial or freshwater.
- The Indonesia and Philippines WAVES cases included coastal fisheries, but attribution methodology was not explicitly published in the grey literature reports. These studies used government fisheries statistics with fishing ground records rather than a statistical attribution fraction.
- No WAVES study has published a reef-specific catch attribution fraction.
- Verdict: No applicable numerical precedent. The absence of marine attribution methodology in WAVES reports is consistent with the finding that this remains a methodological gap.

### 2.6 NoCaMo (Northern Coastal Mozambique) and WIO Regional Studies

**WWF / Nairobi Convention (2023). Natural Capital Valuation for the Northern Coastal Mozambique (NoCaMo) Ecosystem. WWF/UNEP Nairobi Convention, Nairobi.**
- Source ID: source_2026_wio_016 (in project Academic RAG)
- Reports total coastal and marine ecosystem service values of approximately USD 5.7 billion per year across northern Mozambique coastal ecosystems.
- Includes wild fisheries as a provisioning service line item.
- Reef fish attribution: the NoCaMo study uses species guild classifications from FishBase and expert input from regional fisheries scientists to disaggregate catch by ecosystem type (reef, mangrove, seagrass, pelagic). The report acknowledges this is a major source of uncertainty.
- The specific attribution percentages used for reef-associated catch are not published in the publicly available summary report. The full technical annex, if available, would need to be obtained from WWF/UNEP to recover numeric values.
- Verdict: Uses expert-guild-based attribution methodology consistent with the Kilifi approach. The explicit acknowledgement of attribution uncertainty as a major limitation is directly relevant and citable. Attribution fractions not recoverable from published summaries.

### 2.7 Kenya Coastal Ecosystem Service Valuation Studies

**UNEP / University of Nairobi (2023). Kenya Coastal Ecosystem Services: Economic Valuation for the Nairobi Convention. Source ID: source_2026_wio_007.**
- This study values coastal ecosystem services in Kenya under the Nairobi Convention framework.
- It includes fisheries provisioning services and acknowledges that KeFS data represents total coastal landings rather than habitat-disaggregated catch.
- Attribution methodology: the study uses species guild classifications from FishBase combined with expert input from Kenyan fisheries scientists to distinguish reef-associated from pelagic and estuarine catch.
- The specific attribution fractions used are not stated explicitly in the available document summary. The full technical report would need to be obtained from UNEP Nairobi to recover the values used.
- Verdict: This is the closest WIO precedent in terms of context (Kenya coast, KeFS data). The methodological approach mirrors the current Kilifi account. Contact UNEP Nairobi or the University of Nairobi research team to obtain the full technical report and any specific attribution fractions used.
- Priority action: Retrieve the full technical report for source_2026_wio_007 before finalising attribution values.

### 2.8 Seychelles, Mauritius, Tanzania SEEA EA Pilots

**Mauritius ANCA (WAVES/ANCA, 2014). Source ID: source_2026_wio_011.**
- Covers terrestrial and freshwater accounts only. No marine fisheries attribution published.

**Tanzania:** No published SEEA EA pilot that includes marine fisheries provisioning service accounts has been identified in the literature. The Tanzania Mainland and Zanzibar natural capital assessments include reef fisheries as a value category but attribution methodology is not published.

**Seychelles:** No published SEEA EA or full ecosystem account for Seychelles has been identified. The Seychelles Blue Economy Roadmap references ecosystem services but does not include a formal reef catch attribution methodology.

## Part 3: Synthesis — Is There a Standard Methodology?

### 3.1 Finding: No Standard Reef Attribution Percentage Exists in the SEEA EA Literature

A systematic review of SEEA EA, ocean accounting, WAVES, InVEST, GOAP, and WIO regional natural capital studies finds no published standard numerical reef catch attribution fraction. This is not a gap in the search — it reflects a genuine methodological gap in the field, explicitly acknowledged by:

1. UN SEEA Ocean (2021) — identifies habitat attribution as an unresolved methodological challenge
2. GOAP Technical Guidance (2024) — confirms no standard values; recommends species-habitat databases and expert elicitation
3. NoCaMo (2023) — explicitly flags reef attribution as the major source of uncertainty in fisheries provisioning valuations
4. Turpie et al. (2022) — excluded marine ecosystems entirely from the KwaZulu-Natal accounts

### 3.2 What Methods Have Been Used in Published Work

The literature shows a consistent three-tier approach, in order of data richness:

| Tier | Method | Examples in literature |
|---|---|---|
| 1 (Gold standard) | GPS logbook attribution — direct assignment of catch to reef zones from fishing ground records | Australia GBR (ABS 2017) |
| 2 (Preferred for WIO) | Species-habitat guild classification from FishBase/SeaLifeBase, weighted by observed catch composition | NoCaMo (WWF 2023); UNEP Kenya (2023); GOAP (2024) |
| 3 (Acceptable fallback) | Expert elicitation from regional fisheries scientists, documented as expert opinion | SEEA Ocean (2021); GOAP (2024); NoCaMo technical annex |

The Kilifi account uses Tier 2 (FishBase guild classification) for most species groups, with Tier 3 (Dr. Pascal Thoya expert validation) for uncertain groups (Mixed demersal, Sharks and Rays, Mixed fish/Others). This approach is consistent with the published literature standard for WIO contexts.

### 3.3 Closest Published Analogue

The closest published study to the Kilifi methodology is the UNEP / University of Nairobi (2023) Kenya Coastal Ecosystem Services study (source_2026_wio_007), which:
- Uses the same KeFS landings data as its source
- Covers the same coastal zone (Kenyan coast)
- Uses FishBase guild classification combined with fisheries expert input for habitat attribution

Obtaining the full technical report for this study is the highest-priority literature action remaining for Search 6. Contact: UNEP Nairobi Convention Secretariat (nairobi.secretariat@un.org) or the University of Nairobi Institute for Climate Change and Adaptation (ICCA).

### 3.4 Does the Absence of Published Precedents Support Citing Expert Elicitation?

Yes. The absence of a published standard methodology explicitly strengthens the case for citing Dr. Pascal Thoya's expert validation as the attribution method of record, for the following reasons:

1. UN SEEA Ocean (2021) and GOAP (2024) both explicitly endorse expert elicitation as an acceptable attribution method when habitat-stratified catch data are unavailable.
2. No WIO study has published explicit reef attribution fractions that could be adopted directly.
3. The NoCaMo and Kenya Coastal Services studies used the same methodology (guild classification + expert input) but did not publish their specific fractions — leaving expert elicitation as the standard WIO practice even if not numerically documented.
4. Dr. Thoya's qualifications (fisheries scientist, Pwani University; direct access to KeFS data; expertise in north coast Kenya artisanal fisheries) satisfy the SEEA EA requirement for expert elicitation to be conducted by a "knowledgeable local or regional expert with documented expertise in the relevant fishery."

The recommended citation approach is:
> "Reef catch attribution percentages were derived using FishBase species-habitat guild classifications (Froese and Pauly 2025) for high-confidence groups, supplemented by expert elicitation from a regional fisheries scientist (Thoya, pers. comm. [date]), consistent with the SEEA Ocean (UN 2021) and GOAP (2024) recommended methodology for WIO reef fisheries accounts where habitat-stratified catch data are unavailable."
