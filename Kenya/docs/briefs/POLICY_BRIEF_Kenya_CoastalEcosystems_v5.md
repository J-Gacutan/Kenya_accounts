# Coastal Ecosystems of the Western Indian Ocean: Evidence from Kenya's First Multi-Ecosystem Condition Accounts

**Series:** SEEA EA Ecosystem Condition Accounts -- Kenya Pilot
**Geographic scope:** Kilifi County, Kenya (Kanamai to Malindi MPA)
**Accounting period:** 2024-2025
**Status:** Version 5 -- Expert review revision (Round 2)
**Date:** April 2026

---

**Headline findings:** Kenya's first multi-ecosystem condition accounts reveal four results that demand immediate policy attention. (1) Coral recruitment is critically low -- at less than 5% of the lower reference bound for healthy Western Indian Ocean reefs, the reef's capacity to self-repair after bleaching is at risk. (2) Mangroves have declined 5.6% in area despite two-thirds of the estate falling within protected areas, demonstrating that spatial protection alone is not preventing loss. (3) Protected areas show consistently better ecosystem condition than unprotected sites across both coral reef and mangrove ecosystems -- the investment case for management is quantifiable. (4) Seagrass meadows are in generally good condition, with Ngomeni recording 8 species and over 90% cover, among the highest in the WIO region. What follows is the evidence base, the governance implications, and the finance opportunity.

## CORE NARRATIVE

---

### Page 1 -- Situation and Headline Findings

**The Western Indian Ocean at a Crossroads**

Coastal and marine ecosystems across the Western Indian Ocean support the livelihoods of tens of millions of people in nine coastal states from Somalia to South Africa. Coral reefs, seagrasses, and mangroves deliver fisheries, coastal protection, nursery habitat, and carbon storage -- services that underpin both national economies and local food security. Yet these ecosystems are under compounding pressure: coral bleaching events are intensifying, mangrove systems face encroachment and hydrological disruption, and seagrass meadows remain largely unmonitored. For governments and investors alike, the critical gap is not ambition -- it is evidence.

Kenya's Kilifi County pilot has produced the first multi-ecosystem condition accounts for coastal ecosystems in the country, applying the UN System of Environmental-Economic Accounting -- Ecosystem Accounting (SEEA EA) framework across three ecosystem types: photic coral reefs (M1.3), seagrass meadows (M1.1), and mangroves (MFT1.2). The accounts cover 17 to 25 sites depending on ecosystem, spanning approximately 50 kilometres of coastline from Kanamai to Malindi Marine Park. These are not an academic exercise -- they are an institutional infrastructure investment that provides a consistent, reproducible evidence base for ocean governance and nature finance decisions.

**Coral Reefs: Bleaching Resolution, but Recruitment Crisis**

Coral reefs in Kilifi County are in moderate condition following the 2023-24 global bleaching event. Surviving colonies have recovered pigmentation: bleached coral fell from 6.6% to 0%, and healthy colonies rose from 76.9% to 94.8% between November 2024 and July 2025. However, this physiological recovery must be distinguished from ecological recovery -- live hard coral cover declined from 40.0% to 21.9% over the same period due to colony mortality, and natural restoration of cover to pre-bleaching levels will take years to decades.

Fish biomass has improved from 438.8 to 647.5 kg/ha, but remains at 56% of the WIO conservation target of 1,150 kg/ha. Crown-of-Thorns Starfish are absent throughout, and disease prevalence is zero.

The most consequential finding is the recruitment deficit. Coral recruit density stands at 0.010 to 0.054 recruits per square metre -- substantially below the 1 to 3 recruits per square metre reported for healthy WIO reefs. Without recruits, the reef cannot replace bleaching-killed colonies through natural recovery. The survey methodology (belt transect counts) is more likely to undercount than overcount small recruits relative to published reference studies that use dedicated quadrats, meaning the true deficit is likely larger, not smaller, than reported here. Full details, confidence ratings, and site-level data are in Annex A.

**Seagrass: Good Condition, Provisional Baselines**

Seagrass meadows across 10 accounting areas produce composite condition indices in the moderate-to-high range (0.69 to 0.94). Ngomeni is the standout site: 8 species and over 90% cover. These are the first standardised seagrass condition accounts for Kilifi County. All seagrass reference levels carry LOW confidence because WIO-specific benchmarks are not yet formally established; the indices are directional guidance pending peer review. Full site-level data in Annex A.

**Mangroves: Declining Despite Protection**

Mangrove extent totals 5,742 ha, down from 6,083 ha in the GMW 2020 baseline -- a net loss of 341 ha (5.6%) over five years. Two-thirds (66.3%) of the current estate falls within protected areas. The extent decline despite majority PA coverage demonstrates that spatial protection alone is not preventing net loss.

Mangrove condition ranges from moderate (Mida Creek, CI 0.27-0.52) to degraded (Kilifi Creek, CI 0.24-0.30). Kilifi Creek is the priority restoration area, though the condition indices diagnose the degree of degradation, not its cause -- a separate causal assessment of anthropogenic disturbance versus hydrological alteration is required before specific management interventions can be designed. Condition indices are provisional pending peer review of reference levels.

**Protected Areas: Measurable Performance Differential**

In July 2025, Watamu No-Take MPA recorded a composite coral reef condition index of 0.794, compared to 0.635 in unprotected sites. For mangroves, Mida Creek Marine National Reserve recorded CI 0.455 versus 0.370 for unprotected sites -- a 23% differential consistent with the reef pattern. These are the first standardised condition comparisons between protection status categories in Kilifi County. The composite CIs for protected and unprotected groups pool partially different indicator sets due to data availability, so the differentials should be interpreted as directional rather than as a precise management-attributable effect. Geomorphological differences, survey timing, and enforcement compliance levels are additional uncontrolled confounders that must be addressed before attributing the differentials to protection status alone.

### Page 2 -- What This Means for Ocean Governance

**The Governance Gap**

Kenya has a strong legal and policy architecture for ocean management: the Fisheries Management and Development Act (FMDA) 2016, the Environmental Management and Coordination Act (EMCA), the Wildlife Conservation and Management Act 2013, and the Kenya National Blue Economy Strategy (NBES) 2025-2030 collectively provide broad mandates to manage, monitor, and restore coastal ecosystems. Kenya's Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) 2030 includes coastal resilience as a priority adaptation pathway. The National Biodiversity Strategy and Action Plan (NBSAP) is aligned with the Global Biodiversity Framework (GBF) Target 3 (30x30).

The gap is not in mandate -- it is in measurement. None of these instruments currently has defined condition thresholds that trigger management action. The FMDA 2016 authorises fisheries closures and access restrictions but does not specify the ecosystem condition levels at which such measures are activated. The Watamu and Malindi MPA management plans do not reference condition indices as performance metrics. The Kenya National Bureau of Statistics (KNBS) does not yet have standardised environmental accounts for coastal ecosystems. The SEEA EA accounts produced by this pilot provide the missing measurement layer -- designed to be reproduced annually, reported to KNBS, and used to trigger pre-defined management responses.

**The Cost of Inaction**

The accounts quantify what is at stake. Fisheries resource rent attributable to reef and coastal ecosystems in Kilifi County is estimated at USD 2.41 million to USD 5.17 million per year (see Page 3). If fish biomass continues to decline -- a trajectory that the recruitment deficit makes plausible -- this economic value erodes directly. Blue carbon stock in the mangrove estate represents approximately 11.0 to 11.8 million tonnes CO2e. The observed loss rate of approximately 68 ha per year, if sustained, implies ongoing carbon release that both accelerates climate change and forecloses future carbon credit revenue. Inaction is not cost-free; it is a measurable economic loss.

**Tiered Recommendations**

0 to 6 months (Quick Wins):

Embed condition index scores (coral CI, mangrove CI, seagrass CI) into the annual performance reports of Watamu MPA and Malindi Marine Park. This requires no new funding -- only a template revision. Responsible: Director General, Kenya Wildlife Service, and Director of Fisheries; output: revised annual performance report template; timeline: next reporting cycle; resource: existing KWS/KFS budget.

Formally register the Kilifi County accounts with KNBS as an experimental ecosystem account under the SEEA EA framework. Under the Statistics Act (Cap 112), KNBS technical committee review may be required before formal registration. Responsible: PS, State Department of Environment, via letter to Director General, KNBS; output: formal acknowledgement letter and KNBS registration memo; timeline: within 90 days; resource: no new budget required.

Table a Sustainable Blue Economy Action Plan (SBEAP) KPI proposal that uses coral fish biomass (target trajectory to 1,150 kg/ha), coral recruitment rate, mangrove composite CI, and seagrass composite CI as headline ecosystem health indicators under the NBES 2025-2030 framework. Responsible: PS, State Department of Blue Economy; output: SBEAP KPI addendum; timeline: within 60 days; resource: existing operational budget.

The coral recruitment finding, while requiring methodological confirmation, is sufficiently concerning to warrant precautionary review under existing FMDA 2016 powers. Responsible: Director General, Kenya Fisheries Service; output: 60-day desk review of sedimentation and wastewater discharge permits in the Kilifi coastal zone, with findings reported to the PS, State Department of Blue Economy; timeline: within 30 days of brief receipt.

Embed condition indices in the Kilifi County Integrated Development Plan (CIDP) environmental indicators. Responsible: County Executive Committee Member for Environment, County Government of Kilifi; output: CIDP indicator revision; timeline: next CIDP review cycle.

1 to 2 years (Institutional Embedding):

Amend FMDA 2016 operational guidelines to include condition-linked management triggers. A practical first step is to define a provisional threshold -- for example, coral fish biomass below 400 kg/ha OR recruitment below 0.5 recruits per square metre -- that triggers a mandatory review of seasonal closures, gear restrictions, or no-take zone boundaries. This requires a ministerial circular issued by the Cabinet Secretary for Blue Economy and Fisheries under FMDA 2016 Section 5, with engagement of the Fisheries Advisory Council (FMDA Section 10). The Council's current terms of reference should be reviewed to confirm they cover ecosystem condition thresholds.

Designate the Kenya Marine and Fisheries Research Institute (KMFRI) as the technical custodian of the national SEEA EA coastal accounting methodology, with an annual reporting protocol to KNBS feeding into the Kenya Integrated Natural Capital Accounting Programme. KMFRI has permanent survey capacity in Mombasa and a Kilifi field station, making it the natural institutional home for sustained monitoring.

Direct the National Environment Management Authority (NEMA) to incorporate SEEA EA coastal condition indices into the statutory State of the Environment Report cycle under EMCA. NEMA's environmental licensing function should also be engaged: coastal development permits issued under the Kenya Maritime Authority Act 2006 do not currently reference SEEA EA condition account thresholds as a permit condition or post-permit monitoring requirement.

Engage the Nairobi Convention Secretariat not only as a regional coordination mechanism for methodology sharing, but in the context of Kenya's obligations under the Protocol Concerning Protected Areas and Wild Fauna and Flora in the Eastern African Region (ratified by Kenya), which applies to Watamu and Malindi.

2 to 5 years (Systemic Change):

Establish a national coastal accounting programme, replicating the Kilifi pilot in at least two additional counties -- Lamu (mangrove and seagrass priority) and Mombasa (high-pressure reef priority) -- by 2028. County-level implementation requires engagement with the County Governors, County Departments of Environment and Natural Resources, and alignment with county CIDPs.

Commission a formal PA effectiveness analysis to test whether the observed Watamu-versus-unprotected condition differential holds after controlling for site characteristics, enforcement compliance, and geomorphology. This would provide the evidentiary basis for future PA expansion decisions under GBF Target 3.

Position the SEEA EA accounts as Kenya's national evidence base for SDG 14.2 and SDG 14.4 reporting.

### Page 3 -- Unlocking Blue Finance

**The Finance Opportunity**

The Kilifi County accounts quantify three categories of economic value relevant to private sector finance: fisheries resource rent, blue carbon stock, and ecosystem-linked credit risk reduction.

**Fisheries Resource Rent**

Fisheries resource rent in Kilifi County -- the economic surplus generated by reef and coastal fisheries above the cost of fishing -- is estimated at USD 2.41 million to USD 5.17 million per year (2024 sensitivity range across reef attribution fractions of 35-50% and fishing cost ratios of 55-70%). The central estimate for 2024, using the actual species-mix attribution fraction of 32.8%, is USD 3.22 million per year; the sensitivity grid central cell (40% reef, 62% cost) yields USD 3.49 million per year. The five-year trend from 2020 to 2024 shows broadly positive growth, from USD 1.93 million in 2020 to the 2024 range of USD 2.41 million to USD 5.17 million.

All resource rent figures are PROVISIONAL pending validation of the ecosystem attribution fraction by Dr. Thoya (targeted for Q3 2026), and should not be cited in investment or planning documents until that validation is complete. The species-mix figure (USD 3.22 million) is the primary estimate for interim policy use; the sensitivity grid central cell (USD 3.49 million) is provided for methodological transparency.

NOTE: Reef attribution fraction refers to the proportion of Kilifi County fisheries catch attributable to reef, seagrass, and mangrove ecosystems as opposed to offshore and pelagic sources. The 35-50% range is bounded by the conservative estimate (accounting for seagrass overlap in mixed demersal species) and the UNEP Nairobi Convention 2023 estimate (demersal reef fish at approximately 50% of coastal production).

**Risk disclosure:** The resource rent estimate depends on maintaining or improving ecosystem condition. Fish biomass at 647.5 kg/ha is at 56% of the WIO conservation target. If biomass trends downward -- a risk that current recruitment deficits make plausible -- the economic thesis for blue finance reverses. Furthermore, the resource rent reflects current catch levels, which may exceed maximum sustainable yield given fish biomass at 56% of the conservation target. The sustainable resource rent at MSY would provide a more conservative and appropriate basis for long-term blue finance instrument structuring. This risk is not a reason to withhold investment; it is the primary rationale for investment in MPA management and ecosystem restoration.

**Blue Carbon Stock**

Blue carbon stock in the Kilifi County mangrove estate is estimated at 3.0 to 3.2 million tonnes of carbon (5,742 ha at 520 to 560 Mg C per hectare from WIO reference literature, Go Blue/UNEP). Converted to CO2-equivalent, this represents approximately 11.0 to 11.8 million tonnes CO2e.

To provide directional context on the distinction between stock value and annual credit flow: the observed loss rate of approximately 68 ha per year, if sustained and verified under a VM0033 project, would generate a gross annual avoided-loss credit potential on the order of USD 1-3 million per year, before buffer pool requirements (typically 10-20%), leakage deductions, and verification costs. This is material but substantially smaller than the total stock notional value of USD 110-294 million at current voluntary market prices (USD 10-25 per tonne CO2e; WIO mangrove credits have typically transacted in the lower half of this range in 2025-2026). The stock figure is not equivalent to annual credit generation potential.

Seagrass carbon density in Kilifi County is 171 to 220 Mg C per hectare (upper bound consistent with published WIO/Indo-Pacific values; Howard et al. 2017). Total seagrass extent has not yet been mapped. Seagrass extent mapping is the highest-value near-term data investment for completing the total blue carbon stock estimate.

**Blue Carbon Pathway (VCS VM0033)**

The mangrove and seagrass systems in Kilifi County may be eligible for carbon credit generation under VCS VM0033 (Tidal Wetland and Seagrass Restoration). VM0033 includes methodological modules for both ecosystem types. However, combining mangrove and seagrass in a single PDD adds complexity -- the carbon accounting modules use different biomass equations, default carbon density values, and uncertainty tiers -- and is subject to higher validator scrutiny. Project developers should seek Verra-accredited validation body guidance on the most efficient project boundary design for the Kilifi site configuration.

The Kilifi situation (net extent loss with majority PA coverage) is primarily an avoided-loss scenario. Avoided-loss and restoration activities have different additionality tests, baseline methodologies, and credit permanence requirements under VM0033 -- the project design should be framed accordingly.

What the current accounts provide: condition baselines at site level, a mangrove extent account with multi-period change detection, a reproducible monitoring protocol, site-level indices that can inform additionality arguments, and a governance track record (Mida Creek performance differential versus Kilifi Creek).

What remains required: below-ground biomass and soil carbon measurements, a formally validated additionality argument, a Project Design Document (PDD) filed with Verra, a leakage buffer calculation, and an independently verified MRV protocol. Several WIO mangrove projects have successfully navigated these requirements.

**Blue Bond Opportunity**

Kenya has already issued a green bond at sovereign level. The accounts are applicable to ICMA-aligned bond frameworks, providing use-of-proceeds reporting (specific ecosystem areas financed, measured in condition indices) and impact reporting (annual change in CI scores). The accounts support structuring at sovereign, county, or SPV level.

The primary remaining barrier for blue bond issuance is not data availability but obligor creditworthiness and institutional structuring capacity at county or sovereign level. Engagement with a DFI such as AfDB or IFC for pre-structuring advisory support is the critical next action. Kilifi County's sub-national creditworthiness has not been established in public markets; sovereign or DFI-backed structures may be more feasible initially.

For DFI investment officers and ESG fund managers, the accounts reduce a critical due diligence barrier: the absence of standardised, reproducible baseline data. An investor co-financing MPA management or mangrove restoration in Kilifi County can now quantify ecosystem credit risk using a recognised international framework rather than bespoke survey data.

**GCF and GEF Alignment**

The Green Climate Fund's "Oceans and Coastal Zones" funding window prioritises projects that integrate SEEA-aligned accounting into national reporting systems. The condition data -- particularly the bleaching mortality and recruitment crisis findings -- provide a credible adaptation rationale. Two pathways apply: a Readiness and Preparatory Support Programme grant (achievable in 1-2 years through an existing accredited entity such as UNDP or UNEP) and a full GCF project proposal (3-4 year timeline, requiring a national implementing entity with GCF accreditation).

The GEF Biodiversity and Land Degradation focal areas align directly with the PA effectiveness findings and GBF Target 3 measurement needs.

**TNFD Alignment**

For private sector companies with operations or supply chains dependent on Kilifi County coastal ecosystems, the accounts support the Locate and Evaluate steps of the TNFD LEAP process. The accounts contribute to TNFD-aligned disclosure but do not by themselves constitute full TNFD compliance; companies would need to integrate them into broader dependency and impact assessment workflows.

**The Ask**

Three policy decisions would substantially increase the bankability of the Kilifi County evidence base:

First, formal registration of the SEEA EA accounts with KNBS as an official experimental account. This is the minimum institutional endorsement threshold for ICMA-aligned bond frameworks and reduces perceived political risk in nature finance transactions referencing the accounts.

Second, inclusion of condition index thresholds in the revised Watamu and Malindi MPA management plans. This creates a regulatory anchor for blue bond covenant compliance -- investors can reference a statutory management trigger rather than relying on voluntary commitments.

Third, commission a below-ground biomass and soil carbon measurement campaign for Kilifi County mangrove and seagrass systems. Without soil carbon measurements, the blue carbon pathway cannot advance to PDD stage. Additionally, mapping seagrass extent is the highest-value near-term data investment for the full blue carbon stock estimate and unlocking the full scope of private finance opportunity.

In parallel, a formal PA effectiveness evaluation -- as recommended under the 2-5 year systemic change tier above -- would provide the evidentiary foundation for future PA expansion decisions and 30x30 reporting, further strengthening the investment case.

CI calculation note: The CI for live hard coral cover uses a 30% reference (good condition threshold). Sites above 30% receive CI = 1.0 (capped); sites below 30% receive CI = value/30. The mean across sites produces the reported CI. In November 2024, most sites exceeded 30% (CI = 1.0), but Ras Kitau at 12.9% pulled the mean below 1.0, yielding 0.905. Fish biomass CI is calculated similarly: value/1,150 per site, capped at 1.0, then averaged. Fish species richness CI uses a period-specific maximum observed value as the reference (20 spp in Nov 2024; 19.5 spp in Jul 2025), which means the two period CIs are not strictly comparable -- an apparent improvement from 0.629 to 0.728 partly reflects a shift in the reference denominator, not only a change in measured richness. Exact normalisation parameters are documented in the R analysis scripts.

Recruitment method note: Recruits were counted within belt transects. Belt transects of standard width (5 m) systematically undercount small recruits relative to dedicated quadrat surveys (typically 1 m2). Cross-study comparability with published reference values is assumed but not formally validated; if anything, the true deficit is likely larger, not smaller, than reported.

Notes: Cover decline from 40% to 22% reflects mortality from the 2023-24 global bleaching event (surveys approximately 15 months post-peak), not a new independent stressor. Post-bleaching successional processes may continue. Bleaching resolution (return of zooxanthellae) is distinct from ecological recovery (restoration of live cover and structural complexity); the latter requires successful recruitment and will take years to decades under favourable conditions.
