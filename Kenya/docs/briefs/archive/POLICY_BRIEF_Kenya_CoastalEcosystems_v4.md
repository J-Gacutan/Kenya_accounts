# Coastal Ecosystems of the Western Indian Ocean: Evidence from Kenya's First Multi-Ecosystem Condition Accounts

**Series:** SEEA EA Ecosystem Condition Accounts -- Kenya Pilot
**Geographic scope:** Kilifi County, Kenya (Kanamai to Malindi MPA)
**Accounting period:** 2024-2025
**Status:** Version 4 -- Full results integration
**Date:** March 2026

SCOPE NOTE: These accounts include ecosystem condition accounts (SEEA EA Table 5.3) and a mangrove extent account (SEEA EA Table 5.1). Seagrass and coral reef extent accounts and ecosystem services flow accounts are planned for subsequent accounting periods.

## CORE NARRATIVE

### Page 1 -- Situation and Headline Findings

**The Western Indian Ocean at a Crossroads**

Coastal and marine ecosystems across the Western Indian Ocean support the livelihoods of tens of millions of people in nine coastal states from Somalia to South Africa. Coral reefs, seagrasses, and mangroves in this region deliver fisheries, coastal protection, nursery habitat, and carbon storage -- services that underpin both national economies and local food security. Yet these ecosystems are under compounding pressure: coral bleaching events are intensifying, mangrove systems face encroachment and hydrological disruption, and seagrass meadows remain largely unmonitored. For governments and investors alike, the critical gap is not ambition -- it is evidence.

Kenya's Kilifi County pilot has produced the first multi-ecosystem condition accounts for coastal ecosystems in the country, applying the UN System of Environmental-Economic Accounting -- Ecosystem Accounting (SEEA EA) framework across three ecosystem types: photic coral reefs (M1.3), seagrass meadows (M1.1), and mangroves (MFT1.2). The accounts cover 17 to 25 sites depending on ecosystem, spanning approximately 50 kilometres of coastline from Kanamai to Malindi Marine Park. Together they provide a consistent, reproducible evidence base for ocean governance and nature finance decisions.

**What the Accounts Reveal**

Across all three ecosystems, the accounts reveal qualitative patterns of resilience, stress, and recovery -- coral reefs show bleaching impact with recovery signals, seagrass shows generally good condition, and mangroves range from moderate to degraded. These patterns are summarised below; numeric condition indices across ecosystem types reflect ecosystem-specific reference levels and should not be directly compared.

Coral reefs in Kilifi County are in moderate condition following a significant bleaching event. Fish biomass stands at 647.5 kg/ha (July 2025, HIGH confidence), up from 438.8 kg/ha in November 2024, but still well below the Western Indian Ocean conservation target of 1,150 kg/ha. Fish species richness has improved to 13.3 species per transect (MEDIUM confidence). Live hard coral cover declined from 40.0% in November 2024 to 21.9% in July 2025, falling below the 30% threshold for good condition (MEDIUM confidence). This decline reflects mortality from the 2023-24 global bleaching event -- the July 2025 surveys were conducted approximately 15 months post-peak -- rather than a new independent stressor. The recovery signal is clear: bleached coral colonies fell from 6.6% in November 2024 to 0% by July 2025, and the proportion of healthy coral colonies rose from 76.9% to 94.8%. Crown-of-Thorns Starfish are absent throughout, and disease prevalence is zero (both CI 1.0 across all surveys).

However, one finding stands out as a forward-looking risk signal that demands policy attention: coral recruitment is critically low throughout the accounting period. Recruit density was 0.010 recruits per square metre in November 2024 and 0.054 recruits per square metre in July 2025 -- both substantially below the 1 to 3 recruits per square metre reported in WIO reef literature for healthy systems (LOW-MEDIUM confidence; survey-method dependent -- direct comparison assumes methodological equivalence with published reference studies). Without recruits, the reef cannot replace bleaching-killed colonies through natural recovery. Pending methodological equivalence confirmation with published WIO reference studies, coral recruitment represents a significant forward-looking risk signal that warrants monitoring and precautionary management response.

Seagrass meadows across 10 accounting areas in Kilifi County produce provisional composite condition indices in the moderate-to-high range (0.69 to 0.94), noting that all seagrass reference levels carry LOW confidence ratings and these indices should be treated as directional only. Ngomeni is the standout site: 8 seagrass species were recorded in the 2024 survey, representing high local species richness for the WIO region, and total cover reaches 90.75%. The accounts cover 3,040 records from a 2024 baseline. A critical caveat applies: all seagrass condition indicators carry LOW confidence ratings because WIO-specific reference levels for seagrass condition are not yet formally established. The indices are provisional and should be treated as directional guidance rather than definitive benchmarks pending peer review of reference values.

Mangroves across four accounting areas (Mida Creek, Marereni, Ngomeni, Kilifi Creek) show LOW-MEDIUM confidence condition, with composite indices ranging from 0.236 (Kilifi Creek, Mtongani site) to 0.522 (Mida Creek, Kidundu site). Kilifi Creek shows the lowest condition values across the entire mangrove account. The accounts are based on 2,095 tree records across 38 plots. As with seagrass, reference levels are provisional; mangrove condition indices should be treated as indicative. Mida Creek consistently produces the highest condition indices among mangrove areas, consistent with its Ramsar site designation and associated management -- though site characteristics and creek morphology may also contribute to this differential.

Mangrove extent in the Kilifi County study area totals 5,742 ha (2025 classification), down from 6,083 ha in the GMW 2020 baseline -- a net loss of 341 ha (5.6%) over five years, with gross turnover substantially higher (589 ha of additions against 928 ha of reductions). Two-thirds (66.3%) of the current mangrove estate falls within protected areas, dominated by Watamu Marine National Park (1,763 ha). The extent decline despite majority PA coverage indicates that protection status alone is not preventing net loss -- a finding that reinforces the need for condition-linked management triggers alongside spatial protection.

Protected area performance: in July 2025, Watamu No-Take MPA recorded coral cover of 23.8% and a composite condition index of 0.794, compared to 19.2% cover and a composite CI of 0.635 in unprotected sites. For mangroves, Mida Creek Marine National Reserve sites recorded a composite condition index of 0.455, compared to 0.370 for unprotected sites -- a 23% differential consistent with the reef pattern. The accounts provide the first standardised condition comparison between protection status categories in Kilifi County, noting that geomorphological and temporal differences between sites should be addressed before attributing condition differentials to protection status alone.

### Page 2 -- What This Means for Ocean Governance

**The Governance Gap**

Kenya has a strong legal and policy architecture for ocean management: the Fisheries Management and Development Act (FMDA) 2016, the Environmental Management and Coordination Act (EMCA), the Wildlife Conservation and Management Act 2013, and the Kenya National Blue Economy Strategy (NBES) 2025-2030 collectively provide broad mandates to manage, monitor, and restore coastal ecosystems. Kenya's Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) 2030 includes coastal resilience as a priority adaptation pathway. The National Biodiversity Strategy and Action Plan (NBSAP) is aligned with the Global Biodiversity Framework (GBF) Target 3, which calls for 30% of ocean areas to be effectively conserved and managed by 2030.

The gap is not in mandate -- it is in measurement. None of these instruments currently has defined condition thresholds that trigger management action. The FMDA 2016 authorises fisheries closures and access restrictions but does not specify the ecosystem condition levels at which such measures are activated. The Watamu and Malindi MPA management plans do not currently reference condition indices as performance metrics. The Kenya National Bureau of Statistics (KNBS) and the National Integrated Monitoring and Evaluation System (NIMES) do not yet have standardised environmental accounts for coastal ecosystems. The result is that a quantitative measurement framework has not yet been linked to official reporting cycles -- the accounts produced by this pilot provide that missing link.

The SEEA EA accounts produced by this pilot provide the missing measurement layer. They are designed to be reproduced annually, reported to KNBS, and used to trigger pre-defined management responses. They are not an academic exercise -- they are an institutional infrastructure investment.

**Tiered Recommendations**

0 to 6 months (Quick Wins):

The fastest return on investment is embedding the existing accounts into current reporting cycles without requiring new legislation. The Kenya Wildlife Service and Kenya Fisheries Service should embed condition index scores (coral CI, mangrove CI, seagrass CI) into the annual performance reports of Watamu MPA and Malindi Marine Park. This requires no new funding -- only a template revision. Responsible: Director General, Kenya Wildlife Service and Director of Fisheries; output: revised annual performance report template; timeline: next reporting cycle; resource: existing KWS/KFS budget.

The Ministry of Environment should formally register the Kilifi County accounts with KNBS as an experimental ecosystem account under the SEEA EA framework, establishing a precedent for national adoption. Responsible: PS, State Department of Environment, via letter to Director General, KNBS; output: formal acknowledgement letter and KNBS registration memo; timeline: within 60 days; resource: no new budget required.

The State Department of Blue Economy should table a Kenya National Blue Economy Strategy (NBES) 2025-2030 KPI proposal that uses coral fish biomass (target: 1,150 kg/ha), coral recruitment rate, and mangrove condition index as headline ecosystem health indicators. The coral recruitment finding, while requiring methodological confirmation, is sufficiently concerning to warrant precautionary action under existing FMDA 2016 powers. Responsible: Commissioner of Fisheries; output: 60-day KFS desk review of existing sedimentation and wastewater discharge permits in the Kilifi coastal zone, with findings reported to the State Department of Blue Economy; timeline: within 30 days of brief receipt; resource: existing KFS operational budget.

1 to 2 years (Institutional Embedding):

The FMDA 2016 operational guidelines should be amended to include condition-linked management triggers. A practical first step is to define a provisional threshold -- for example, coral fish biomass below 400 kg/ha OR recruitment below 0.5 recruits per square metre -- that automatically triggers a review of seasonal closures, gear restrictions, or no-take zone boundaries in the affected area. This does not require primary legislation; it requires a ministerial circular and engagement with the Fisheries Advisory Council. An annual SEEA EA reporting protocol should be established between the Kenya pilot team and KNBS, feeding into the Kenya Integrated Natural Capital Accounting Programme. The Nairobi Convention Secretariat should be engaged as a regional coordination mechanism to share methodology and pilot Kenya's approach across other WIO states.

2 to 5 years (Systemic Change):

A national coastal accounting programme should be established, replicating the Kilifi pilot in at least two additional counties -- Lamu (mangrove and seagrass priority) and Mombasa (high-pressure reef priority) -- by 2028. The GBF Target 3 (30x30) commitments in Kenya's NBSAP need a measurement framework to demonstrate effectiveness. The SEEA EA condition accounts, stratified by protection status, are the most credible quantitative basis for such demonstration. A formal PA effectiveness analysis, drawing on the condition differential observed between Watamu No-Take MPA (CI 0.794) and unprotected sites (CI 0.635), should be commissioned to test whether the observed differential is attributable to protection status after controlling for site characteristics. This would provide the evidentiary basis for future PA expansion decisions. SDG 14.2 (sustainably manage and protect marine ecosystems) and SDG 14.4 (regulate harvesting to achieve maximum sustainable yield) both require national reporting to the UN. The Kenya SEEA EA accounts are the most robust national evidence base currently available for both indicators.

### Page 3 -- Unlocking Blue Finance

**The Finance Opportunity**

The Kilifi County accounts quantify three categories of economic value that are of direct relevance to private sector finance: fisheries resource rent, blue carbon stock, and ecosystem-linked credit risk reduction.

Fisheries resource rent in Kilifi County -- the economic surplus generated by reef and coastal fisheries above the cost of fishing -- is estimated provisionally at USD 2.06 million to USD 5.66 million per year (2024 full sensitivity range across reef attribution fractions of 30-55% and fishing cost ratios of 55-70%). The central estimate for 2024 is USD 3.22 million per year, but this figure is PROVISIONAL pending final validation of the ecosystem attribution fraction by Dr. Thoya and should not be cited as a confirmed value in investment documents. The attribution fraction refers to the proportion of Kilifi County fisheries catch attributable to reef, seagrass, and mangrove ecosystems as opposed to offshore and pelagic sources -- this fraction must be validated by Dr. Thoya before the monetary estimate can be used for policy or investment purposes. Both the central estimate and the full sensitivity range are provisional and should not be cited in investment or planning documents until Dr. Thoya's attribution validation is complete. The range is provided here for directional context only. Attribution validation is expected to be complete before brief finalisation; confirmation of status should be sought from Dr. Thoya before external distribution. The five-year trend from 2020 to 2024 shows broadly positive growth, from USD 1.93 million in 2020 to the 2024 range, suggesting that the coastal fishery has economic momentum that improved ecosystem condition could amplify.

Risk disclosure: The fisheries resource rent estimate depends on maintaining or improving ecosystem condition. Fish biomass at 647.5 kg/ha is at 56% of the WIO conservation target. If biomass trends downward -- a risk that current recruitment deficits make plausible -- the economic thesis for blue finance reverses. This risk is not a reason to withhold investment; it is the primary rationale for investment in MPA management and ecosystem restoration. Investors and government budget officers should treat the rent figures as conditional on continued or improving reef health.

**Blue Carbon Stock**

Blue carbon stock in the Kilifi County mangrove estate is estimated at 3.0 to 3.2 million tonnes of carbon (using the 2025 extent of 5,742 ha and per-hectare densities of 520 to 560 Mg C per hectare from WIO reference literature). Converted to CO2-equivalent, this represents approximately 11.0 to 11.8 million tonnes CO2e. At current voluntary carbon market prices of USD 10 to 25 per tonne CO2e, the notional stock value is USD 110 million to USD 294 million -- though this is a total stock value, not an annual credit flow, and credit generation would require verified additionality and a formal project design document under VCS VM0033 or equivalent. The mangrove extent account shows a net loss of 341 ha (5.6%) between 2020 and 2025, implying that carbon stock is actively declining -- a trend that strengthens the additionality argument for avoided-loss crediting but also signals urgency for management intervention.

Seagrass carbon density in Kilifi County is 171 to 220 Mg C per hectare, but total seagrass extent has not yet been mapped. Total seagrass carbon stock cannot be calculated until extent data are available. Seagrass extent mapping is the highest-value near-term data investment for completing the total blue carbon stock estimate.

**Blue Carbon Pathway**

The mangrove and seagrass systems in Kilifi County may be eligible for carbon credit generation under VCS VM0033 subject to additionality demonstration -- the accounts provide baseline evidence that supports but does not confirm eligibility. This methodology covers both mangrove restoration and seagrass restoration -- a single PDD can address both ecosystem types where sites are geographically contiguous and managed under a single project proponent -- an efficiency advantage where these conditions are met.

What the current accounts provide towards a VM0033 project: condition baselines for both mangrove and seagrass at site level, a mangrove extent account with multi-period change detection (6,083 ha opening to 5,742 ha closing), a reproducible monitoring protocol, site-level indices that can inform additionality arguments, and a governance track record (Mida Creek performance differential versus Kilifi Creek).

What remains required to reach credit issuance: below-ground biomass and soil carbon measurements (not currently in the accounts), a formally validated additionality argument, a Project Design Document (PDD) filed with Verra, a leakage buffer calculation, and an independently verified monitoring, reporting, and verification (MRV) protocol. These are significant but well-defined data and process gaps, and several WIO mangrove projects have successfully navigated them. The SEEA EA accounts reduce pre-feasibility costs by providing the baseline evidence layer that most project developers must otherwise commission from scratch.

**Blue Bond Opportunity**

Kenya has already issued a green bond at sovereign level. The Kilifi County accounts support blue bond structuring at multiple levels -- sovereign (Kenya Treasury), county (Kilifi County Government), or through a special purpose vehicle -- each with different risk profiles, investor bases, and covenant requirements. The issuance level is not determined by this brief; financial advisors and the relevant issuing authority would determine the appropriate structure. The accounts are applicable as impact reporting metrics for all three structures.

The accounts are directly applicable to two covenant structures that ICMA-aligned bond frameworks typically require: use-of-proceeds reporting (specific ecosystem areas financed, measured in condition indices) and impact reporting (annual change in CI scores across reef, seagrass, and mangrove accounting units).

For DFI investment officers and ESG fund managers evaluating coastal nature-based investments in Kenya, the accounts reduce a critical due diligence barrier: the absence of standardised, reproducible baseline data. An investor co-financing MPA management or mangrove restoration in Kilifi County can now quantify ecosystem credit risk using a recognised international framework rather than bespoke survey data.

**GCF and GEF Alignment**

The Green Climate Fund's "Oceans and Coastal Zones" funding window prioritises climate adaptation and mitigation in coastal ecosystems, with a preference for projects that integrate SEEA-aligned accounting into national reporting systems. The Kilifi pilot is directly aligned with this preference. The condition data -- particularly the bleaching mortality and recruitment crisis findings -- provide a credible adaptation rationale for GCF financing of reef restoration and MPAs. A national coastal accounting programme (as proposed under the 2-5 year recommendation above) would be a strong candidate for GCF readiness funding or a Readiness and Preparatory Support Programme grant.

**TNFD Alignment**

For private sector companies with operations or supply chains dependent on Kilifi County coastal ecosystems -- including fishing enterprises, aquaculture, coastal tourism, and port logistics -- the Kilifi accounts support the Locate and Evaluate steps of the TNFD LEAP process. They provide asset-level ecosystem condition data that can be used to identify nature-related dependencies and impacts in the Kilifi coastal zone. It is important to note that the accounts contribute to TNFD-aligned disclosure but do not by themselves constitute full TNFD compliance; companies pursuing TNFD disclosure would need to integrate the accounts into their broader dependency and impact assessment workflows.

**The Ask**

Three policy decisions would substantially increase the bankability of the Kilifi County evidence base:

First, formal registration of the SEEA EA accounts with KNBS as an official experimental account. This signals institutional endorsement to investors and reduces perceived political risk in nature finance transactions referencing the accounts.

Second, inclusion of condition index thresholds in the revised Watamu and Malindi MPA management plans. This creates a regulatory anchor for blue bond covenant compliance -- investors can reference a statutory management trigger rather than relying on voluntary commitments.

Third, commission a below-ground biomass and soil carbon measurement campaign for Kilifi County mangrove and seagrass systems, building on the now-completed GMW 2020 extent baseline (5,742 ha mangrove). Without soil carbon measurements, the blue carbon pathway cannot advance to PDD stage. Additionally, mapping seagrass extent is the highest-value near-term data investment for completing the total blue carbon stock estimate and unlocking the full scope of private finance opportunity.
