# What Are Kilifi County's Coastal Ecosystems Worth to Fisheries?

**Series:** SEEA EA Ecosystem Service Accounts -- Kenya Pilot
**Geographic scope:** Kilifi County, Kenya (Malindi, Watamu, Kilifi Creek, Takaungu, Ngomeni)
**Reference period:** 2020-2024 (constant 2024 USD)
**Status:** PROVISIONAL -- pending Dr. Thoya attribution validation (targeted Q3 2026)
**Date:** April 2026

---

**Headline:** Coral reef and coastal ecosystems in Kilifi County generate an estimated USD 2.4 million to USD 5.2 million per year in fisheries resource rent -- the economic surplus that the ecosystem contributes to the value of landed fish. At the individual level, this represents approximately USD 230 per fisher per year, or roughly 20% of a typical fisher's net earnings. This value is at risk: fish biomass stands at 56% of the WIO conservation target, coral recruitment is critically low, and current catch levels may exceed sustainable yield. Every year that ecosystem condition remains below potential, Kilifi County forgoes economic value that better-managed reefs could generate.

---

### Page 1 -- The Fisheries Economy of Kilifi County

**Scale of the Fishery**

Kilifi County is Kenya's largest marine artisanal fishery by volume, accounting for 30.5% of national marine catch in 2024. Total county marine catch was 12,095 metric tonnes, generating KES 3.1 billion (approximately USD 23.0 million) in ex-vessel revenue from five principal landing sites along approximately 50 kilometres of coastline. The fleet is predominantly artisanal: approximately 80% of vessels are non-motorised (sails and paddles), and 48% are self-owned or family-owned. An estimated 14,000 fishers depend directly on this resource, with a typical fisher earning approximately KES 400 per day net (USD 3.1). At the 2024 central rent estimate of USD 3.22 million, the ecosystem contribution is approximately USD 230 per fisher per year -- roughly 20% of net earnings.

The fishery is open-access in practice, with limited effective effort controls outside the Watamu and Malindi Marine Protected Areas. This matters for interpreting the resource rent: in open-access fisheries, competitive entry tends to erode economic surplus over time as effort expands. The positive rent observed here may partly reflect short-run conditions rather than a durable equilibrium -- which is why the management actions recommended on Page 2 are a prerequisite for this value to be sustained.

**What the Accounts Show**

The Kilifi County SEEA EA pilot has produced the first resource rent time series for a Kenyan coastal fishery. Resource rent is the economic surplus generated by fishing after deducting all costs of production (labour, fuel, gear, vessel depreciation, and a normal return on capital), isolating the ecosystem's contribution. Under the UN SEEA EA framework, resource rent is the standard valuation method for wild fish provisioning services.

Note on trend: the apparent growth in catch and revenue from 2020 to 2024 reflects both genuine catch expansion and improved landing site monitoring coverage (the proportion attributable to each is not yet quantified). The trend should not be read as a pure production increase.

**Sensitivity Range**

The sensitivity grid uses a fixed 40% attribution as a scenario analysis tool; the actual data-derived attribution is 32.8% for 2024. The reef attribution fraction varied from 33% to 45% across 2020-2024. The decline from approximately 39% to 33% over this period may reflect a genuine compositional shift toward pelagic and estuarine species as reef fish deplete -- if confirmed by Dr. Thoya, this would reinforce the overfishing signal.

The cost ratio range (55-70%) reflects published estimates for Kenyan artisanal fisheries (Munga et al. 2014; Mills et al. 2011; Cinner et al. 2009), adjusted for the predominantly non-motorised Kilifi fleet. These cost studies use data from 2006-2012; if the true 2024 cost ratio is at the upper bound of 70% due to wage growth and fuel price inflation, the central estimate falls to approximately USD 2.6 million per year. Fleet ownership data is extrapolated from Kwale County (Nyawade et al. 2021) pending Kilifi-specific survey data.

### Page 2 -- What Is at Risk and What Should Be Done

**The Ecosystem-Fisheries Link**

Resource rent depends on ecosystem condition. Three signals from the Kilifi County condition accounts directly affect the sustainability of this value:

Fish biomass stands at 647.5 kg/ha (July 2025) -- only 56% of the WIO conservation target of 1,150 kg/ha. A healthier reef would support higher sustainable catch and higher rent, but only if recovery is accompanied by effective effort controls. Under open access, increased biomass can attract additional fishing pressure that absorbs the gains, leaving rent unchanged. The management actions below are therefore a prerequisite for the economic upside to materialise.

Coral recruitment is critically low: 0.010 to 0.054 recruits per square metre, compared to 1 to 3 in healthy WIO reefs. Without recruitment, the reef cannot recover structural complexity that juvenile fish depend on.

Current catch levels may exceed maximum sustainable yield. The resource rent estimate values current extraction, not sustainable extraction. The sustainable resource rent at MSY would be more conservative and a more appropriate basis for long-term investment planning. A full overfishing assessment is a prerequisite for any investment-grade use of these figures.

**What This Means for Policy**

First, ecosystem management is an economic investment, not a cost. The difference between current biomass (647.5 kg/ha) and the WIO target (1,150 kg/ha) represents unrealised economic potential that these accounts make visible for the first time.

Second, the FMDA 2016 should incorporate ecosystem condition thresholds that trigger management review. Proposed trigger values for KFS technical review -- for example, fish biomass below 400 kg/ha or coral recruitment below 0.5 recruits per square metre -- would link the condition accounts directly to fisheries management action, operationalised through Beach Management Units (BMUs) at the landing site level. This requires a ministerial circular from the Cabinet Secretary for Blue Economy and Fisheries under FMDA Section 5, following stakeholder consultation with the Fisheries Advisory Council and affected BMUs.

Third, the resource rent provides the economic foundation for blue finance instruments -- PES schemes, blue bonds with fisheries-linked covenants, or GCF-financed MPA management. The Fisheries Levies Trust Fund (established under FMDA 2016) should be assessed as a potential payment mechanism for ecosystem service payments. Current figures should not be used for investment structuring until Dr. Thoya's attribution validation is complete and the overfishing signal is formally assessed.

**Method Summary**

Resource rent = reef-attributed gross revenue x (1 - cost ratio). Reef-attributed revenue is derived by assigning each of 37 KeFS species groups a reef attribution percentage based on FishBase habitat classifications, validated against KeFS 2016 Kilifi family-level catch data (39.1%). Nominal KES values are deflated to constant 2024 KES using GDP deflators, then converted to USD at the 2024 exchange rate (134.82 KES/USD). The cost ratio (central: 62%) reflects published estimates for Kenyan artisanal fisheries, adjusted for Kilifi's predominantly non-motorised fleet. Unreported catch is adjusted at x1.30 based on published estimates for the Kenya coast (McClanahan and Mangi 2004; Samoilys et al. 2017). For non-motorised artisanal vessels, the normal return on capital is treated as negligible and excluded from the cost ratio. Belt transect methodology for coral recruitment may undercount small recruits relative to dedicated quadrat surveys -- the deficit finding is likely larger, not smaller, than reported. Full methodology: `Kenya/docs/accounts/KEN_resource_rent_analysis.md`.
