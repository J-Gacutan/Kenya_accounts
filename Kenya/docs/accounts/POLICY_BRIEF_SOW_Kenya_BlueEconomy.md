# Scope of Work: Kenya Coastal Ecosystems Policy Brief
**Advocating SEEA EA Accounts for Ocean Governance and Blue Finance — Kilifi County, Kenya**

---

## 1. POLICY QUESTIONS

This policy brief addresses three interconnected questions that link ecosystem account findings to Kenya's ocean governance agenda and blue finance priorities.

### Primary PQ1: What Is the Condition and Value of Kenya's Coastal Ecosystems?
*What do SEEA EA accounts reveal about the current condition of Kilifi County's coral reefs, seagrass meadows, and mangroves — and what economic value do these ecosystems provide to fisheries, coastal livelihoods, and the national blue economy?*

- Coverage: Coral reef (M1.3), seagrass (M1.1), mangrove (MFT1.2) condition accounts — all three OUTPUTS AVAILABLE — plan steps not formally signed off
- Geographic scope: Kanamai to Malindi MPA, Kilifi County; 2024–2025
- Data sources: ~11,700 coral/fish records, 3,040 seagrass records, 2,095 mangrove tree records from three independent survey programmes (CCVA/KMFRI, PU-GOAP, COMRED Oceans)
- Services covered: Wild fish provisioning (physical and monetary accounts complete), blue carbon (mangrove + seagrass carbon stocks), coastal protection (qualitative)
- Headline reef findings: Fish biomass CI 0.284 (Nov 2024) rising to 0.357 (Jul 2025) vs WIO target of 1,150 kg/ha (mean biomass: 438.8 kg/ha Nov 2024; 647.5 kg/ha Jul 2025); coral cover declined 40% to 22% between surveys (2024 bleaching impact); bleaching fully resolved by Jul 2025 (6.6% to 0%); coral recruitment critically low throughout (0.01–0.05 recruits/m²); COTS absent; Watamu MPA shows marginally higher coral cover (23.8%) vs unprotected (19.2%)
- Output: Integrated condition dashboard (0–1 indices by ecosystem) + economic value summary

### Secondary PQ2: What Does This Mean for Ocean Governance?
*How can ecosystem condition accounts strengthen marine protected area management, fisheries regulation, and coastal spatial planning across the Kilifi County coastline — and how should Kenya's governance frameworks be updated to institutionalise this evidence?*

- Governance instruments addressed: Fisheries Management and Development Act (FMDA) 2016, Environmental Management and Coordination Act (EMCA), Kenya National Blue Economy Strategy (NBES) 2025–2030, Sustainable Blue Economy Action Plan
- Policy gap: No standardised mechanism currently links reef/seagrass/mangrove condition to MPA management targets, fisheries closures, or marine spatial planning decisions
- Output: Regulatory gap analysis; tiered recommendations by ministry/agency

### Tertiary PQ3: How Can Ecosystem Accounts Unlock Blue Finance?
*What natural capital evidence base do Kenya's coastal ecosystems provide for blue bonds, blue carbon markets, payment for ecosystem services, and donor-aligned conservation finance — and what governance steps are needed to make that evidence bankable?*

- Finance instruments: Blue carbon credits (mangrove + seagrass carbon stocks); blue bonds (State Department of Blue Economy); GCF and GEF ecosystem finance; payment for ecosystem services (fisher livelihood schemes)
- Natural capital anchor: Mangrove blue carbon 526–560 Mg C ha⁻¹ (Kwale and Lamu county precedents); seagrass carbon 171–220 Mg C ha⁻¹; wild fish provisioning service value
- Output: Blue finance readiness assessment; linkage table (ecosystem condition indicator → finance instrument)

## 2. POLICY GAPS & CONTEXT

### 2.1 Ocean Governance Gap
**Condition monitoring not yet integrated into MPA management or fisheries regulation**

- Status quo: Watamu Marine National Park and Malindi MPA operate under Kenya Wildlife Service and KMFRI monitoring frameworks but do not produce annual condition accounts in a standardised format. Fisheries management under the Fisheries Management and Development Act (FMDA) 2016 is production-oriented; ecosystem condition thresholds are not embedded in regulatory trigger points.
- This brief fills: A SEEA EA condition account linking coral reef, seagrass, and mangrove health to existing PA management plans; a proposed mechanism for annual condition tracking as a fisheries management tool.
- Policy relevance: Kenya's NBES 2025–2030 targets "sustainable use of marine and freshwater resources"; SEEA EA accounts provide the measurement infrastructure for that target.
- Impact window: Brief delivered to Kenya State Department of Blue Economy and Kenya Wildlife Service ahead of NBES mid-term review (2027).

### 2.2 Blue Finance Readiness Gap
**Natural capital data exists but is not packaged for finance instruments**

- Status quo: Kenya hosted the 2018 Sustainable Blue Economy Conference (Nairobi Declaration). The country has active blue economy policy ambition but lacks a credible, standardised natural capital evidence base to attract blue bond issuers, carbon market buyers, or GCF programme funding at scale.
- This brief fills: Links SEEA EA condition accounts directly to blue finance readiness criteria. The Kilifi accounts establish the ecological baseline layer required by VCS VM0033, but additional work is needed before credit issuance: below-ground biomass and soil carbon sampling, additionality demonstration, and project design document (PDD) preparation. This SOW identifies the remaining data gaps.
- Finance gap: Blue carbon credit revenue from Kenya's mangrove estate alone (approximately 60,000 ha nationally; ~520–560 Mg C ha⁻¹) could generate significant conservation finance — but only if underpinned by verified, nationally recognised condition accounts. No such accounts exist today.
- Policy relevance: Aligns with Kenya's NBSAP (National Biodiversity Strategy and Action Plan) and GBF Target 3 (30x30 ocean protection), SDG 14.2, and emerging TNFD (Taskforce on Nature-related Financial Disclosures) requirements for institutional investors operating in Kenya's blue economy sectors.

### 2.3 Multi-Ecosystem Integration Gap
**Seascape-scale accounting not yet attempted for Kenya**

- Status quo: Individual ecosystem assessments exist (KMFRI coral reef surveys, Global Mangrove Watch extent mapping, scattered seagrass studies) but have never been integrated into a single, spatially coherent accounting unit under a common framework.
- This brief fills: The Kilifi County dataset is the first to cover coral reef, seagrass, and mangrove conditions simultaneously across an overlapping spatial footprint with fisheries provisioning data. Ngomeni is the anchor demonstration site — the only location in the dataset with all three habitat types plus fisheries overlap.
- Policy relevance: Integrated seascape accounts enable coastal spatial planning decisions (where to prioritise protection, restoration, or sustainable use zoning) that single-ecosystem assessments cannot support.

### 2.4 Institutional Capacity Gap
**SEEA EA is mandated but not yet operationalised in Kenya**

- Status quo: Kenya has engaged with SEEA EA implementation through its NBES 2025–2030 and NBSAP; the Nairobi Convention provides a regional cooperation framework but does not carry specific SEEA EA implementation commitments. The Kenya National Bureau of Statistics (KNBS) is exploring natural capital accounting but has not published coastal ecosystem accounts.
- This brief fills: Demonstrates a replicable SEEA EA workflow for Kenyan coastal ecosystems; provides a template for national scale-up; offers KNBS a Kilifi County case study to anchor the national accounts programme.
- Capacity anchor: Pwani University (Dr. Pascal Thoya, Dr. Juliet Karisa) and COMRED Oceans are the institutional leads; this brief positions those institutions as the technical capacity nodes for Kenya's coastal accounting programme.

## 3. POLICY OBJECTIVES

### Primary Objective
Deliver a policy brief that translates Kilifi County's SEEA EA condition accounts into concrete, actionable recommendations for Kenya's ocean governance frameworks and blue finance instruments, with sufficient technical credibility for uptake by the State Department of Blue Economy, Kenya Wildlife Service, Kenya Fisheries Service, and international finance partners.

### Secondary Objectives
1. **Governance integration:** Propose specific amendments or additions to existing regulatory instruments (NBES 2025–2030 implementation plan, Watamu and Malindi MPA management plans, Fisheries Management and Development Act (FMDA) 2016 operational guidelines) that embed ecosystem condition thresholds into management triggers
2. **Blue finance pathway:** Map the Kilifi County data to blue carbon crediting standards (Verra VCS, Plan Vivo, or Gold Standard), blue bond covenant criteria, and GCF/GEF finance eligibility requirements; identify the next steps to close remaining data gaps
3. **Regional positioning:** Frame Kenya's accounts within the Western Indian Ocean regional context; position the Kilifi case study as a model for Tanzania, Mozambique, and Madagascar to adopt — enabling a coherent WIO regional blue economy evidence base
4. **Institutional anchoring:** Recommend governance home for the annual account cycle (KNBS, KMFRI, or joint ministry committee); specify data collection and reporting responsibilities

**E. Regional and International Framing**
- [included] WIO regional context: Position Kilifi accounts within Western Indian Ocean regional trends (declining coral cover post-2023–24 bleaching event; mangrove loss rates; seagrass status); Kenya as a leading early adopter
- [included] TNFD / ISSB alignment: SEEA EA accounts contribute to the TNFD LEAP process (specifically the Locate and Evaluate steps) and provide nature-related risk data relevant to ISSB-aligned disclosures, but do not constitute full TNFD compliance, which requires additional business dependency and impact assessment — relevant to international investors with Kenyan blue economy exposure
- [included] SDG 14 and post-2020 GBF linkage: Map condition indices to SDG 14.2 (marine ecosystem management), 14.4 (sustainable fishing), and GBF Target 3 (30x30 ocean protection)

## 9. TARGET AUDIENCES

The brief has two confirmed primary reader profiles that must be served simultaneously and with equal weight:

### Primary Audience A: Senior Government Decision Makers

- **Who:** Cabinet-level and senior officials; Principal Secretaries in the State Department of Blue Economy, Ministry of Environment, and Kenya Wildlife Service; likely to share the brief with Parliamentary finance or environment committees
- **What they need:** A clear accountability signal — what the data reveals about ecosystem condition, what it costs to do nothing, and which specific regulatory instruments need to change or be created; language of national strategy, budget lines, and ministry mandates
- **Tone:** Authoritative, concise, action-oriented; one policy recommendation per paragraph; no jargon; connect each data point to a governance decision

### Primary Audience B: Private Sector Sustainable Nature Finance Representatives

- **Who:** Investment officers at development finance institutions (IFC, DEG, FMO), ESG-oriented fund managers with Kenya blue economy exposure, voluntary carbon market project developers, blue bond structuring advisors (e.g., Standard Chartered, Citi sustainability units), TNFD early adopters
- **What they need:** A credible natural capital evidence base; specific carbon stock values, ecosystem condition trends, and data quality tiers that satisfy due diligence thresholds; a clear pathway from current account outputs to investable instrument (blue bond covenant, carbon credit MRV, nature performance bond)
- **Tone:** Precise, evidence-led, risk-framed; specific numbers with confidence levels; realistic about data gaps; connect condition data to financial materiality

### Dual-Audience Design Principle

Each page of the brief must deliver value to both audiences without requiring them to read different documents. Policy language dominates the opening and recommendations; natural capital and finance framing dominates the evidence sections and the blue finance readiness matrix. The brief succeeds when a PS can use it in a budget meeting and a DFI investment officer can cite it in an investment committee paper.

### Secondary Audiences

- Pwani University and COMRED Oceans (institutional partners; capacity building)
- WIOMSA and Nairobi Convention Secretariat (regional dissemination)
- Conservation NGOs active in Kenya coast (WCS, WWF, CORDIO East Africa)
- KNBS (pathway to integrating accounts into national statistics)
