## 1. Conceptual Framework

Wild fish provisioning services are the ecosystem contributions to the growth of fish and other aquatic biomass that are caught in uncultivated production contexts. Under the SEEA EA, this is a final ecosystem service valued at exchange prices.

Resource rent measures the economic surplus generated by fishing activities after deducting all costs of production at their opportunity costs. It isolates the ecosystem's contribution to the monetary value of landed fish, separate from the contributions of labour, produced capital, and intermediate inputs.

### Interpretation

- A **positive** resource rent indicates the reef ecosystem is generating economic value above what labour and capital could earn in their next-best use.
- A **negative** resource rent would indicate overcapitalisation or excess labour -- fishing costs exceed the ecosystem's productive capacity.
- Resource rent is bounded by gross revenue from above and by zero from below in a well-functioning fishery. In open-access fisheries under heavy exploitation, rent may be driven toward zero (Gordon-Schaefer bioeconomic equilibrium).

### Calculation structure

```
  Total reef-attributed revenue (gross)
- Intermediate costs (fuel, gear consumables, maintenance, ice)
- Labour costs (crew wages or opportunity cost of fisher labour)
- Return on fixed capital (normal return on vessel and gear investment)
- Consumption of fixed capital (depreciation of vessels and gear)
= Resource rent (ecosystem contribution)
```

In a Tier 1 estimate where individual cost components are unavailable, the aggregate cost-to-revenue ratio substitutes for the itemised deduction:

```
Resource Rent = Gross Revenue x (1 - cost_ratio)
```

### 7.1 Published cost ratio estimates

| Source | Region | Cost ratio | Data year |

| Munga et al. 2014 (WIOJMS 13(1):57-70) | Kilifi-Malindi | 60--70% | ~2012 |
| Mills et al. 2011 (Ambio 40(7):747-756) | Kilifi-Malindi-Mombasa | 55--65% | ~2008 |
| Cinner et al. 2009 (MEPS 370:1-10) | Kenya-Tanzania-Mozambique | 50--70% | ~2006 |

### 7.2 Structural validation

| Evidence | Source | Finding | Implied cost ratio |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Revenue-sharing model | UNEP Nairobi Convention 2023 | Gross revenue split 1/3 middleman, 1/3 boat maintenance, 1/3 fishers | ~67% |
| Tajiri system | Degen et al. 2010 | Vessel/gear owner takes 20--50% of daily catch | 53--83% |
| Gross-to-net cross-check | McClanahan and Kosgei 2025; Onyango et al. 2021 | Gross USD 7.0/fisher/day vs net KES 400/day (~USD 3.1) | ~56% (self-owned) |
| Fleet motorisation | FAO Kenya; Nyawade et al. 2021 | 80% non-motorised (sails 43%, paddles 40%); fuel share ~5% of revenue | Lowers fuel component |
| Vessel ownership | Nyawade et al. 2021 (Kwale) | 48% self/family-owned, 30% rented with pay, 13% rent-free, 9% employer | Blended cost ratio |

### 7.3 Cost component decomposition (estimated)

The aggregate cost ratio represents the sum of individual cost components. Direct data for Kilifi is not available (Tier 2 would require fisher surveys), but the fleet-weighted structure can be estimated:

| Cost component | Literature estimate (% of gross revenue) | Fleet-weighted estimate (accounting for 80% non-motorised) | Source |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Labour (crew share or opportunity cost) | ~40% | ~40% | WIO literature; validated by McClanahan and Kosgei 2025 living wage analysis |
| Fuel | ~20% (if fully motorised) | **~5%** | Only 20% of vessels use engines; fleet-weighted = 20% x 20% + 0% x 80% = 4% |
| Gear and vessel maintenance | ~10% | ~8--10% | UNEP-NC 2023 (1/3 to maintenance); lower for non-motorised |
| Capital depreciation | ~5% | ~4--5% | Non-motorised vessels have lower replacement cost |
| Other (ice, handling, licences) | ~3--5% | ~3% | Minimal for non-motorised; most fish sold fresh at landing site |
| **Total** | **~78--80%** | **~60--63%** | -- |

Note: The literature estimates (78--80%) assume a fully motorised fleet, which overestimates costs for Kenya's predominantly non-motorised artisanal fishery. The fleet-weighted decomposition yields ~60--63%, consistent with the 62% central estimate.

### 7.4 Cost ratio sensitivity bounds

| Estimate | Cost ratio | Basis |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Low (optimistic) | 55% | Self-owned, non-motorised vessels; minimal middleman extraction |
| **Central** | **62%** | Blended fleet: ~50% self-owned (55%) + ~50% tajiri-operated (67%) |
| High (conservative) | 70% | Tajiri-operated motorised vessels; Munga 2014 upper bound |

### 7.5 Fuel price temporal adjustment

Kenya fuel prices approximately doubled between 2020 and 2024. However, because 80% of the fleet is non-motorised, the fleet-weighted fuel share is only ~5% of gross revenue. Impact:

| Year | Super petrol, Mombasa (KES/litre, est.) | Fuel price index (2012 base) | Adjusted cost ratio |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 2020 | ~94 | 0.82 | 0.611 |
| 2021 | ~119 | 1.03 | 0.622 |
| 2022 | ~148 | 1.29 | 0.635 |
| 2023 | ~185 | 1.61 | 0.651 |
| 2024 | ~184 | 1.60 | 0.650 |

The shift is ~2.5 percentage points across the full period -- within the sensitivity bounds. The 62% central estimate is used for all years.
