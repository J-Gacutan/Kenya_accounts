## 1. Overview & Conceptual Framework

### 1.1 Wild Fish Provisioning Service Definition

The **wild fish provisioning service** is the ecosystem's contribution to food and livelihood security through:

1. **Fishery production** — Commercial and subsistence catch of reef fish species
2. **Income generation** — Market value of landed fish for fisher households
3. **Food security** — Protein availability for local communities

**Distinction from Nursery Service:**
- **Fisheries nursery** (regulating service): Ecosystem enhances fish *density* (juvenile survival); measured as enhanced biomass
- **Wild fish provisioning** (provisioning service): *Actual catch* harvested by fishers; measured as realized landings

> Both services depend on fish biomass but measure different aspects: nursery measures ecosystem *contribution*, while provisioning measures human *use/extraction*.

## 3. Catch Estimation (Provisional)

### 3.1 Top-Down Estimate (Condition Account Method)

**Assumptions:**
1. Harvestable fish (catchable families only): ~60% of total biomass = 0.6 × 1,929 = 1,157 kg/ha
2. Reef area: 500 ha (from extent account, pending confirmation)
3. Total harvestable biomass: 1,157 × 500 = 578,500 kg
4. Annual harvest rate: 20% of standing biomass (sustainable estimate)
5. **Annual catch: 578,500 × 0.20 = 115,700 kg/yr**

**Sanity check:**
- 27 sites surveyed; ~50 km of reef coastline estimated (rough)
- If 115,700 kg/yr spread over 50 km = 2,314 kg/km/yr
- This is plausible for artisanal fishery with low–moderate industrial presence

### 3.2 Bottom-Up Estimate (Fisher Survey Method) — REQUIRED

**Data needed (priority acquisition):**

1. **Landing site catch surveys** (n=2–3 sites, 3 months, weekly monitoring):
   - Record species, weight (kg), gear type, landing date
   - Interview fishers: effort (hours/day), number of trips, crew size
   - **Target:** 100–200 landing events; estimate total catch by extrapolation

2. **Fisher cost survey** (n=20–30 fishers, 1-time survey):
   - Labour (crew wages)
   - Fuel costs (petrol for motors)
   - Gear maintenance/replacement
   - Vessel depreciation (if applicable)

3. **Market price survey** (parallel to nursery service skill):
   - Current prices (USD/kg or MGA/kg) by species family
   - Seasonal variation (if any)
   - Wholesale vs. retail prices

## 4. Economic Valuation

### 4.1 Resource Rent Method

**Resource Rent = Gross Revenue − Total Costs**

$$\text{Resource Rent} = \sum \left(\text{Catch}_i \times \text{Price}_i\right) - \sum(\text{Labour, Fuel, Capital Costs})$$

where *i* indexes species or species groups.

## 8. Integration with Other Accounts

### 8.1 Relationship to Fisheries Nursery Service (Regulating)

| Aspect | Nursery Service | Provisioning Service |
|---|---|---|
| **What it measures** | Ecosystem *capacity* (enhanced biomass from nursery effect) | Ecosystem *use* (actual catch by humans) |
| **Metric** | Enhanced biomass (kg/ha) | Annual landings (kg/yr) |
| **Method** | LRR comparison (nursery vs. non-nursery) | Catch surveys + market sales |
| **Value** | ~$61,087/yr (from service valuation) | ~$87,563/yr net profit (resource rent) |
| **Relationship** | Nursery creates the condition supporting provisions | Provisioning realizes the nursery's potential |
| **Double-counting risk** | Moderate — both use fish biomass but measure different aspects | Mitigate: clearly separate "enhanced density" vs. "actual catch" |

### 8.2 Links to Condition Account

- Fish biomass (1,929 kg/ha) → Harvestable pool
- Species richness (55 spp/site) → Catch diversity
- Functional group composition → Market value diversity (herbivores low value, carnivores high value)

### 8.3 Links to Extent Account

- Reef area (ha) → Total harvestable biomass × area
- Change in extent → Direct impact on service supply

## 10. Alternative Valuation Methods (For Comparison)

### 10.1 Replacement Cost (Aquaculture)

**Concept:** Value = Cost to replace wild catch with farmed fish

**Pros:** Technology-independent; sustainable alternative valuation

**Cons:** Madagascar has limited aquaculture; costs highly speculative; not well-suited for artisanal context

**Application:** Limited; useful only for sensitivity analysis

### 10.2 Benefit Transfer (Global Unit Values)

**Concept:** Use published USD/ha/yr for tropical reef fisheries from other regions

**Example:** van Beukering et al. (2003): Caribbean coral reef fish ≈ $50–100/ha/yr (inflation-adjusted ~$80–150/ha/yr 2026)

**Pros:** Rapid; uses peer-reviewed estimates

**Cons:** High transfer error; ignores Madagascar price/cost structures; not recommended for policy accounts

### 10.3 Export Value Method

**Concept:** Value = Market price × quantity exported (ignores domestic + subsistence use)

**Pros:** Directly observable in trade data

**Cons:** Undervalues food security; misses local market value

**Application:** Useful for export-oriented industries (tuna) but inappropriate for subsistence-dominated artisanal fisheries
