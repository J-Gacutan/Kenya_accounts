# Madagascar Ocean Policy Update 2024–2030

## Executive Summary

Madagascar's ocean governance landscape underwent a fundamental restructuring in 2024–2025, transitioning from fragmented sectoral management to an integrated "Blue Economy" framework. This document updates the RAG schema with comprehensive policy information covering the strategic evolution, new institutional frameworks, and critical international commitments entered into during this period.

**Key Milestones:**
- **2024–2025:** Adoption of National Blue Economy Strategy (SNEB) and National Investment Plan (PNIEB)
- **September 25, 2024:** Madagascar signs BBNJ (High Seas Treaty)
- **September 23, 2025:** Madagascar ratifies BBNJ
- **January 17, 2026:** BBNJ entry into force — Madagascar gains mandate for high-seas governance
- **September 2023:** Nairobi Convention ICZM Protocol adopted in Antananarivo
- **Late 2025:** OCPP Phase 1 completes marine megafauna legislative review (5 → 94 protected species)

---

## National Context & Paradox

**Geopolitical Challenge:** Madagascar possesses extraordinary marine natural capital (1,141,120 km² EEZ; 3rd-largest reef system; 2% global mangroves) yet ranks 5th globally in multidimensional poverty. The state must balance:
- **Immediate socio-economic needs** (75% of coastal population dependent on marine resources; extreme poverty)
- **Long-term ecosystem preservation** (natural capital degradation threatens national stability; annual cyclone losses = 4.8% GDP)
- **Revenue generation** (IUU fishing costs 14.4–23.7 million USD/year; sustainable finance target = 100 million USD by 2030)

## Part I: The Blue Economy Paradigm Shift

### National Blue Economy Strategy (SNEB) — Comprehensive Policy Framework

**Status:** Adopted 2024 | Implementation: 85% (operationalization ongoing via PNIEB)

**Overarching Definition:**
> "The optimization of the economic, responsible, and sustainable exploitation of maritime and freshwater resources."

**Strategic Objective:** Decouple economic growth from environmental degradation, utilizing the ocean as a "macro-economic stabilizer."

#### Five Strategic Axes of SNEB

| Axis | Objective | Key Programs | Implementation Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Axe 1: Blue Governance Improvement** | Strengthen institutional framework & cross-sectoral coordination | Resolving overfishing/pollution; enhancing monitoring; defining legal scope | Legal framework complete; coordination mechanisms in place |
| **Axe 2: Natural Resource Valuation** | Sustainable exploitation for food security & energy | Marine ecosystem protection; sustainable aquaculture; marine tourism | Valuation frameworks developing; alternative livelihoods pilots active |
| **Axe 3: Infrastructure & Service Development** | Reduce coastal isolation; resilient maritime infrastructure | Marine Spatial Planning (PEM); port connectivity; heritage management | PEM framework being developed |
| **Axe 4: Water & Renewable Energy Access** | Leverage oceanic/hydrological resources | Water retention; marine renewable energy (MRE); thermal energy | Early-stage exploration |
| **Axe 5: Climate Resilience & Adaptation** | Mitigate climate shocks & natural disasters | Restore coastal carbon sinks; integrate adaptation into planning; DRM | Blue carbon initiatives active (Tahiry Honko, Ma Honkô) |

**Strategic Insight:** The five-axis structure deliberately positions the ocean as a risk-reduction mechanism against climate hazards (cyclones cost 4.8% annual GDP) and economic volatility.

### National Investment Plan for Blue Economy (PNIEB) — Operationalization

**Status:** Adopted June 2024 | Implementation: 70%

**Capital Requirements:** Hundreds of millions USD for infrastructure, governance, ecosystem restoration

**Financing Mechanisms:**
- Debt-for-Nature Swaps (DfNS)
- Blue Bonds
- Global Environment Facility (GEF) Strategic Action Programs
- World Bank concessional financing
- EU and UK Blue Planet Fund support
- Critical Ecosystem Partnership Fund (CEPF) — currently updating Ecosystem Profile; 280,000 USD in immediate strategic investments; pathway toward CEPF "graduation"

**Key Programs:**
- Marine Spatial Planning (PEM) framework development
- Port infrastructure enhancement
- Renewable energy projects
- Mangrove restoration (40,000 ha target via Ma Honkô)

## Part II: International Treaty Milestones & Obligations

### BBNJ Treaty Ratification & Entry into Force — Watershed Moment

**Ratification Timeline:**
- **September 25, 2024:** Madagascar signs the BBNJ Agreement
- **September 23, 2025:** Madagascar deposits instrument of ratification
- **January 17, 2026:** BBNJ enters into force globally; Madagascar becomes bound

**Treaty Name (Full):** Agreement under the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea on the Conservation and Sustainable Use of Marine Biological Diversity of Areas Beyond National Jurisdiction

#### Strategic Implications for Madagascar

**Mandate Gained:**
1. **High-Seas MPA Establishment** — Participate in creating protected areas beyond Madagascar's EEZ
2. **Marine Genetic Resources Regulation** — Control access to genetic resources; benefit-sharing mechanisms
3. **Environmental Impact Assessments (EIAs)** — Mandate EIAs for high-seas activities adjacent to Madagascar's waters, offering protection against harmful industrial activity
4. **Capacity-Building Access** — Developing-nation provisions for scientific capability building and technology transfer (critical for Madagascar)

**Operational Gaps (2026 Priority):**
- High-seas MPA nomination procedures
- EIA review mechanisms for adjacent high seas
- Marine genetic resource regulatory framework
- Capacity-building program design

**Expected Outcome:** By COP1 (2026), Madagascar should nominate initial high-seas MPA candidates and establish EIA procedures for adjacent high seas.

**Critical Challenge:** Marine target (~22× increase in MPA coverage) is significantly more ambitious than terrestrial progress.

### Nairobi Convention & Regional Ocean Governance

#### ICZM Protocol (Adopted September 2023, Antananarivo)

**Signatory Countries:** 10 (Comoros, France, Kenya, Madagascar, Mauritius, Mozambique, Seychelles, Somalia, Tanzania, South Africa)

**Standardized Framework Addresses:**
1. **Climate Change Vulnerability** — CCVA toolkit for identifying at-risk communities
2. **Ocean Acidification** — Integrated with Regional Ocean Acidification Action Plan (ROAAP, adopted 2022)
3. **Marine Pollution** — Oil spill preparedness (lesson from 2020 Mauritius incident)
4. **Disaster Risk Reduction** — Integration into coastal planning

**Madagascar's Leadership Role:**
- Hosted Nairobi Convention COP10 (2021)
- Chaired ICZM Protocol negotiation (adopted Sept 2023 in Antananarivo)
- Chairs regional task forces on coral reefs and mangroves
- Participates in CCVA and ocean acidification monitoring

**Implementation Status:** High alignment with domestic Decree 2010-137 (ICZM); gap = community-level CCVA assessments not yet complete in all regions.

#### Regional Ocean Acidification Action Plan (ROAAP) — Adopted 2022

**Focus:** Monitor pH changes in coral reef and mollusk ecosystems; early warning systems

**Relevance to Madagascar:** Coral reefs and mollusks are economically critical; acidification poses significant threat to condition accounts (fish biomass, invertebrate indicators depend on reef health).

## Part III: Institutional & Legal Frameworks (2015–2025)

### Foundation Legislation

#### Law No. 2018-025 — Maritime Zones Under UNCLOS
- **Status:** Foundational; fully implemented
- **Scope:** EEZ (1,141,120 km²), territorial waters (12 nm), contiguous zone, continental shelf
- **Role:** Legal basis for all subsequent ocean governance (provides authority for BBNJ implementation, fisheries management, resources)

#### Decree No. 2017-936 — Blue Economy Institutional Framework
- **Status:** Operational; 90% implementation
- **Key Achievement:** Creation of Ministry of Fisheries and the Blue Economy (MPEB); inter-ministerial coordination with Ministry of Environment (MEDD)
- **Gap:** Some coordination challenges between MPEB and MEDD remain; joint governance structures developing

#### Decree No. 2015-957 — Decentralization & Local Consultation
- **Status:** Active; 75% implementation
- **Innovation:** Creates legal framework for Locally Managed Marine Areas (LMMAs); legitimizes traditional "Dina" (community social codes) when court-ratified
- **Current Network:** 50+ LMMAs operational; targeting 150+ by 2030
- **Benefit:** Bridges national law with traditional norms; enables community enforcement of fishing restrictions and seasonal closures

### Fisheries & IUU Fishing Response

#### National Action Plan for IUU Fishing (PAN-INN) 2025–2029
- **Status:** Adopted January 2025; 75% operational
- **Scope:** Multi-pronged: satellite monitoring, real-time vessel tracking, beneficial ownership identification, PSMA enforcement
- **Infrastructure:** Regional Maritime Information Fusion Center (RMIFC) Madagascar operational; integrated with Seychelles Regional Centre for Operational Coordination
- **Annual Loss:** 14.4–23.7 million USD from IUU (estimated lower bound)
- **Institutional Anchoring:** SADC Monitoring, Control, and Surveillance Fisheries Coordination Centre (MCSCC)

**Strategic Synergy:** RMIFC + Seychelles coordination = sophisticated "maritime domain awareness" enabling joint law enforcement against sea crime.

## Part IV: Biodiversity & Species Protection

### Marine Megafauna Legislative Update (Decree 2006-400, Revised 2025)

**Phase 1 OCPP Review — Completed Late 2025**

**Historic Gap:** Original decree (2006) protected only 5 sea turtle species; 90+ threatened marine species had no legal status.

**Post-2025 Expansion:** Technical consensus reached on 94-species protection framework

| Species Group | Pre-2025 Status | Post-2025 Status | Conservation Impact |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Sea Turtles** | 5 species (absolute protection) | Maintained | Full continued protection |
| **Marine Mammals** | Not specifically listed | Full protection (whales, dolphins, dugongs) | Enables wildlife trafficking prosecution |
| **Sharks & Rays** | Treated as fisheries product | Strict protection for threatened elasmobranchs | Protects against overfishing |
| **Coelacanths** | No protection | Absolute protection | Living fossil status protected |
| **Threatened Bony Fish** | No specific status | CITES Appendix I/II basis | Market-driven threat reduction |

**Operational Support (OCPP Phase 1):**
- Seagrass Monitoring Protocol created
- Management Effectiveness Tracking Tool (METT-4) training for Madagascar National Parks (MNP) rangers
- Ensures MPAs are "functional ecological units" not "paper parks"

**Implementation Impact:** Provides legal basis for MPEB/MEDD to prosecute wildlife traffickers and regulate by-catch in industrial/artisanal fisheries.

## Part V: Blue Carbon & Mangrove Restoration

### Tahiry Honko — World's Largest Community-Led Mangrove Carbon Project

**Location:** Bay of Assassins, Ménabe region

**Specifications:**
- 1,200+ hectares of intact mangrove preservation
- Plan Vivo-verified carbon credits
- Direct income generation for coastal communities
- World's largest community-led initiative

**Model Significance:** Demonstrates integration of climate mitigation with livelihood and coastal protection.

### Ma Honkô — Large-Scale Mangrove Restoration Initiative

**Timeline:** 2022–present (long-term through 2040+)

**Targets:**
- 40,000 hectares of mangrove restoration across Sofia, Melaky, Boeny, Diana regions
- ~20 million tCO₂ sequestration over lifetime

**Scaling Up Ecosystem-Based Adaptation Project (July 2025)**
- **Funding:** 34 million USD
- **Scope:** 3,000 ha mangrove restoration + 2,000 ha degraded watershed restoration
- **Innovation:** Integrates nature-based solutions into municipal planning (Boeny, Ménabe, Diana, Atsimo Atsinanana)
- **Significance:** Builds climate resilience into local development fabric

**Carbon Math:** Halting annual global wetland loss = 0.23 GtCO₂/year reduction. Madagascar's restoration targets contribute significantly to this potential.

## Part VI: Socio-Economic Integration & Just Transition

### ILO Just Transition Framework Applied to Blue Economy

**Status:** Participating framework (not binding treaty, but actively applied)

**Pilot Sector:** Lobster-catching, Southern Madagascar

**Mechanism:** "Inter-professionalization" — bringing together fishers, processors, exporters to:
- Formalize labor relations
- Improve social dialogue
- Create model for broader fishing sector formalization

**Monitoring Tools:**
- Supply and Use Tables (SUT)
- Green Jobs Assessment Models (GJAM)

**Objectives:**
- Estimate job impacts of climate policies
- Identify vocational training needs (women, youth)
- Ensure equitable transition to sustainable sectors

### Alternative Livelihoods Portfolio

**Programs Supporting Ecosystem Integrity While Building Resilience:**

| Livelihood | Ecological Benefit | Community Benefit | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Mangrove Apiculture** | Preserves intact mangrove forests | High-value honey for local/export markets | Expanding |
| **Sea Cucumber Farming** | Reduces pressure on wild stocks | Stable income for village associations | Pilot phase |
| **Mangrove Silk Production** | Reduces timber cutting pressure | Empowers women in textile value chains | Development |
| **Eco-tourism** | Values healthy reefs/forests as recurring asset | Jobs in hospitality, guiding for youth | Growth phase |

**UNEP/GEF Initiative:** Creating 20 new ecosystem-based businesses to build "resilience buffer" against climate shocks.

**Key Insight:** These programs address root cause of degradation — lack of viable economic alternatives for 80% of population in extreme poverty.

## Part VII: Integration with SEEA EA Ecosystem Accounting

### How 2024–2030 Policies Support Extent, Condition, Services Accounting

#### Extent Accounts (Spatial Boundaries)

**Policy Support:**
- Law 2018-025 — Define EEZ boundaries (1,141,120 km²)
- SNEB Axe 3 — Marine Spatial Planning (PEM) framework (under development)
- PAN-INN — Zoning of fishing areas (MPA boundaries, trawl-free zones)
- Nairobi ICZM Protocol — Coastal zone definition & mapping

**Data Availability:**
- MPA boundaries (WDPA database) — 16,097 km²
- High-seas MPA nominations (post-BBNJ entry into force, Jan 17, 2026)
- Ecosystem habitat maps (Sentinel-2 monitoring ongoing)

**2026 Gaps:**
- Full extent mapping not complete; Sentinel-2 processing in progress (Mar 2026)
- High-seas extent additions dependent on BBNJ CoP1 (2026)

#### Condition Accounts (Ecosystem Health)

**Policy Support:**
- Decree 2006-400 (revised 2025) — 94-species protection enables monitoring of megafauna populations
- Seagrass Monitoring Protocol (OCPP support) — Standardized seagrass condition assessment
- Nairobi ICZM Protocol + ROAAP — Ocean acidification monitoring (threat to coral health)
- METT-4 training — Ecosystem effectiveness tracking

**Data Availability:**
- Fish biomass & community composition (27 sites, 5,186 UVC records)
- Invertebrate indicators (COTS, sea urchins, clams)
- Seagrass condition baseline (protocol finalized 2025)

**2026 Gaps:**
- Standardized monitoring not yet mandatory across all MPAs
- Ocean acidification baseline data limited
- Fish size/age structure data incomplete

#### Services Accounts (Economic & Social Benefits)

**Policy Support:**
- SNEB Axe 2 (Natural Resource Valuation) — Resource pricing framework
- Decree 2016-1492 — Landing site data collection authority
- ILO Just Transition — Labor formalization for livelihoods data
- Alternative livelihood programs — Direct income tracking
- Tahiry Honko + Ma Honkô — Blue carbon valuation model

**Data Availability:**
- Fisheries landings (landing site mandate exists; enforcement variable)
- Fisher income (LMMA surveys ongoing)
- Blue carbon credits (Tahiry Honko model replicable)
- Tourism revenue (emerging data from eco-tourism pilots)

**Critical 2026 Gaps:**
- Landing site systematic data collection not enforced consistently (Decree 2016-1492)
- Market price data missing (critical for fisheries provisioning service valuation)
- Tourism visitor data not yet integrated into reef condition service assessments

## Conclusion: The "Polycentric" Ocean Governance Model

Madagascar's 2024–2030 ocean policy represents a sophisticated "polycentric" governance approach integrating:

1. **High-Level International Law** — BBNJ, CBD, Nairobi Convention
2. **Regional Coordination** — Nairobi Convention ICZM Protocol, SAPPHIRE, RMIFC
3. **National Frameworks** — SNEB, PNIEB, PAN-INN, marine legislation
4. **Local Stewardship** — Dina/LMMAs, community-led carbon projects

**Central Paradox Remains:** Balancing immediate poverty (80% extreme poverty) with long-term conservation (natural capital degradation threatens stability) and revenue generation (sustainable finance needs = 100 million USD by 2030).

**Success Metric:** Implementation of the "fundamental balance between conservation and exploitation" — not merely policy adoption, but tangible outcomes for Malagasy people and Western Indian Ocean biodiversity by 2030.
